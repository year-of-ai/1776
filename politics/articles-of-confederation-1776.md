# Articles of Confederation: America's First Constitution

**Category**: Political Events

**Key Figures**: John Dickinson (principal author), delegates from thirteen colonies

## Summary

On June 12, 1776, the Second Continental Congress appointed a thirteen-member committee to draft a plan of confederation binding the colonies into a unified government. **John Dickinson** of Pennsylvania chaired the effort and principally authored the resulting framework. The committee presented their draft to Congress on July 12, 1776—eight days after the Declaration of Independence—beginning months of debate over state sovereignty, representation, western land claims, and the powers of a continental authority. Congress finally adopted the completed Articles on November 15, 1777, and after a contentious four-year ratification process (Maryland was the last state, ratifying on March 1, 1781), the Articles became America's first operational constitution. Though designed as a temporary wartime measure to bind the states in a "league of friendship," the Articles revealed the deep tensions between state autonomy and unified action: a weak confederal Congress lacking taxation power and reliant on state compliance became the template for American government from 1781 to 1789, when it was replaced by the federal Constitution. The 1776 drafting embodied the revolutionary generation's political philosophy—placing sovereignty in the states, limiting executive power, and establishing that legitimate authority derives from the people and states, not a distant monarch.

## Franklin's Earlier Draft: The Articles of Confederation (1775)

The Dickinson committee did not work from a blank slate. **Benjamin Franklin** had submitted an earlier draft confederation plan to Congress in **July 1775**, titled "Articles of Confederation and Perpetual Union." Franklin's 1775 proposal was more radical in some respects and more centralizing in others: it established annual meetings of Congress, provided for proportional representation by population (not equal votes per state), and proposed that new colonies could be admitted. More significantly, Franklin included provisions for collective management of western lands—anticipating precisely the dispute that would delay ratification from 1777 to 1781.

Congress tabled Franklin's 1775 draft without serious debate, as most delegates were not yet prepared to openly discuss formal confederation while still hoping for reconciliation with Britain. By mid-1776, with independence imminent, Dickinson's committee revisited the Franklin draft alongside other materials. The committee ultimately chose equal representation per state (departing from Franklin), retained the "perpetual union" language (from Franklin), and adopted the general architecture of enumerated congressional powers alongside reserved state sovereignty.

The phrase "Articles of Confederation and Perpetual Union" in the final document's title preserved Franklin's 1775 language, though the word "perpetual" was somewhat ironic given that the document proved neither perpetual (replaced in 1789) nor fully a union (states retained extensive sovereignty).

## The Drafting: June–July 1776

On June 12, 1776—one day after Congress named the Committee of Five to draft the Declaration of Independence, and weeks before independence was formally declared (the Lee Resolution passed July 2, the Declaration on July 4)—the Continental Congress appointed a committee of thirteen delegates, one from each state, to draft a constitution for a confederation. The timing reflected the Revolutionary leaders' recognition that military victory alone was insufficient: a legitimate political framework binding the thirteen colonies into a unified nation was necessary to sustain the war effort and establish a new order.

**John Dickinson**, a Pennsylvania delegate, chaired the committee. A lawyer and political theorist who had authored the *Townshend Letters* (1767–1768) criticizing Parliamentary taxation and the Declaration of the Causes and Necessity of Taking Up Arms (1775), Dickinson brought both intellectual rigor and pragmatic political experience to the task. Though he had been reluctant to support independence itself—his caution in June 1776 earned him criticism from more radical delegates—Dickinson was widely respected for constitutional design.

The committee worked rapidly during the summer of 1776, drawing on both English traditions and recent colonial experience. On July 12, 1776, they presented their draft to Congress for debate. The document proposed to establish a confederation of thirteen sovereigns—the united colonies—bound together for common defense and mutual support, while preserving the independence of each state in all matters not delegated to the confederal authority.

## Core Principles: State Sovereignty and Weak Central Authority

The Articles of Confederation embodied a revolutionary political philosophy that **sovereignty resides in the states**, not in a central authority. This reflected both ideological commitment to popular liberty and practical political reality: no state was willing to subordinate itself to a distant continental government, having just escaped submission to a distant monarchy.

### Fundamental Structure

The frame established a unicameral Congress—a single legislative body—in which **each state held one vote regardless of population**. Delegates were appointed by state legislatures (not elected by popular vote) and could be recalled at any time. This equality of states reflected an understanding of the confederation as a voluntary alliance of sovereign bodies, not a representative democracy of individuals.

### Limited Federal Powers

The confederal Congress was granted explicit authority over: military affairs (declaring war, making treaties, raising armies—though states supplied troops and funds); foreign trade and commerce with foreign nations; disputes between states; managing the postal system; and borrowing money. However, the Congress lacked a critical power: **taxation**. It could requisition funds from states, but had no authority to levy taxes itself. This fundamental weakness would undermine the confederation's finances throughout its existence.

### Preserved State Powers

The opening principle declared: "Each state retains its sovereignty, freedom, and independence, and every power, jurisdiction, and right, which is not by this Confederation expressly delegated." States retained control over internal governance, commerce within their borders, and the militia. This radical decentralization reflected the colonists' fear of concentrated power and their recent experience with Parliament's overreach.

## Key Provisions of the Final Text

The final Articles of Confederation, adopted November 15, 1777, comprised thirteen articles. Several provisions defined the document's fundamental character:

**Article I** named the entity "The United States of America"—the first official use of this name in a governing document.

**Article II** declared: "Each State retains its sovereignty, freedom, and independence, and every Power, Jurisdiction, and right, which is not by this Confederation expressly delegated to the United States, in Congress assembled." This article became the philosophical cornerstone of the document and the template for the Tenth Amendment to the U.S. Constitution.

**Article III** established the confederation's purpose: a "firm league of friendship" for "common defence, the security of their liberties, and their mutual and general welfare." The word "friendship" (rather than "union") was carefully chosen to signal that states were cooperating, not merging.

**Article V** fixed representation: each state sent between two and seven delegates to Congress, but cast exactly one vote regardless of delegation size or population. Delegates served one-year terms and could serve no more than three years in any six-year period, a term-limit provision reflecting distrust of entrenched political power.

**Article VIII** addressed the confederation's core fiscal weakness: expenses for common defense and general welfare would be supplied by the states "in proportion to the value of all land within each State" as surveyed and valued by Congress. Crucially, Congress had no enforcement mechanism — it could determine each state's share but could not compel payment.

**Article XIII** required unanimous consent of all thirteen states to amend the Articles — a threshold so demanding that it proved impossible to use, as even one state could block reform. This inflexibility was central to why the Articles were abandoned rather than amended.

## The Congressional Debates: July 1776 – November 1777

The draft presented on July 12, 1776 sparked seventeen months of intensive debate, with Congress deliberating off and on until November 1777. The central conflicts were:

**Representation and Voting**: Small states insisted on equal votes (one per state) to prevent domination by populous colonies like Virginia and Pennsylvania. Larger states sought representation proportional to population. The compromise gave every state one vote, institutionalizing the assumption that states were the constituent units of the confederation, not populations.

**Western Land Claims**: Several states—notably Virginia, North Carolina, Massachusetts, and Connecticut—held colonial charters claiming lands extending to the Pacific Ocean. Others—Maryland, New Jersey, Pennsylvania, Delaware, and Rhode Island—held no western claims. The question of whether these vast western territories would belong to the individual claiming states or to the confederation as a whole threatened to fracture the union. Dickinson's draft assigned western lands to the confederation; southern states objected. The compromise in the final Articles allowed claiming states to retain their lands, but Congress gained authority to settle disputes between states over western boundaries.

**Voting Thresholds**: Several matters required **supermajority votes** (nine of thirteen states). These included declaring war, making treaties, borrowing money, and engaging military forces. This supermajority requirement reflected distrust of simple majorities and the desire to ensure broad consensus on major decisions.

**Duration**: The Articles were adopted "for the duration of the war" but remained in effect beyond the war's end. Congress debated whether the confederation was temporary (lasting only until peace) or permanent. The final version left the question ambiguous, stating the confederation would continue "unless and until altered by the unanimous consent of all the States."

## Adoption and Ratification: November 1777 – March 1781

After seventeen months of revision, Congress adopted the final version of the Articles of Confederation on **November 15, 1777**—nearly a year and a half after Dickinson first presented the draft. The timing coincided with the Battle of Saratoga (October 1777), the first major American military victory, which validated the Continental Congress's political legitimacy.

The Articles then required ratification by all thirteen states—a unanimous consent threshold that reflected the confederation's nature as a voluntary alliance of sovereigns. Ratification proved contentious:

- **Virginia** ratified on December 16, 1777
- By February 1779, twelve states had approved the document
- **Maryland** alone refused, objecting that states with western land claims (particularly Virginia) would profit from vast territories while western lands should belong to the Confederation
- The impasse lasted nearly two years until **December 1780**, when Virginia ceded its western claims to the United States (the transfer was completed in March 1781)
- **Maryland** ratified on **March 1, 1781**, completing the process

The Articles of Confederation officially went into effect on March 1, 1781, establishing the Congress of the Confederation as America's first operating government structure. However, by this date—after five years of military struggle and political argument—the document's weaknesses were already apparent.

## Governance Under the Articles: 1781–1789

The period under the Articles revealed both the document's strengths and its crippling deficiencies:

**What Worked**: The confederation successfully coordinated the final years of the Revolutionary War. Congress continued to function, states cooperated in military efforts, and international recognition of American independence was achieved through the Treaty of Paris (September 1783).

**What Failed**: The central government's lack of taxing power created fiscal chaos. Congress could requisition funds from states, but states often refused to pay. The national debt from the war grew unpaid. Unable to regulate interstate commerce, states imposed tariffs on each other, fragmenting economic unity. Congress lacked power to enforce treaties or regulate foreign commerce effectively. Disputes between states—over borders, commerce, competing land claims—simmered without effective resolution mechanisms.

By the mid-1780s, prominent figures including **George Washington**, **James Madison**, and **Alexander Hamilton** recognized that the confederation was failing. Economic chaos, state disputes, and the inability to raise revenue for defense threatened national survival. This recognition prompted the Constitutional Convention of 1787, which met ostensibly to amend the Articles but instead drafted an entirely new Constitution.

## Significance

The Articles of Confederation represent the first attempt by the thirteen united colonies to constitute themselves as a single political entity while preserving their individual sovereignties. The 1776 drafting and the 1777 adoption embodied the Revolutionary generation's political principles:

1. **Sovereignty of the People and States**: By placing ultimate authority in the states, which derived their power from the people, the framework rejected the idea of absolute central sovereignty.

2. **Limited Government**: The enumeration of Congress's powers reflected distrust of centralized authority and commitment to the principle that government may exercise only delegated powers.

3. **Federalism as Compromise**: The confederation model attempted to balance the need for unified action in military and foreign affairs with the preservation of state autonomy—a balance that proved impossible under the Articles but became the enduring principle of American federalism.

4. **Political Learning**: The Articles' failures directly informed the Constitutional Convention's design. The 1787 Constitution's stronger central government—with taxing power, treaty authority, and executive enforcement—emerged from the lessons of confederation weaknesses.

The Articles thus represent both a culmination of Revolutionary political thought and a stepping stone toward the constitutional framework that replaced them. The 1776 drafting reflected confidence that a weak central authority could unite diverse colonies; the 1789 Constitution reflected the hard-won knowledge that unity required stronger federal power.
