# Virginia Declaration of Rights

**Category**: Political Events
**Key figures**: George Mason, James Madison, Thomas Ludwell Lee, Virginia Convention delegates

## Summary

The Virginia Declaration of Rights was adopted unanimously by Virginia's Fifth Revolutionary Convention at Williamsburg on June 12, 1776 — more than three weeks before the Continental Congress declared independence. George Mason was its principal drafter, preparing an initial framework of ten sections in late May 1776 that a committee of convention delegates then amended before the full convention debated and ratified the final text. Young James Madison played a notable role during the convention debates, successfully proposing the substitution of "equally entitled to the free exercise of religion" for Mason's original language of "toleration" — a change that reframed religious liberty as a fundamental right rather than a government-granted privilege.

The document comprises sixteen articles establishing republican principles and enumerating specific protections. These include the right to trial by jury, confrontation of witnesses, protection against self-incrimination, and safeguards against cruel punishment and unlawful search and seizure. Article 12 declared freedom of the press "one of the greatest bulwarks of liberty." Article 16 guaranteed free exercise of religion. The declaration also articulated popular sovereignty — that "all power is vested in, and consequently derived from, the people" — and the right to reform or abolish inadequate government. A preliminary draft was printed and circulated in early June 1776 before final enactment, and that circulating draft exercised considerable influence on other colonies and on Thomas Jefferson's drafting of the Declaration of Independence.

The Virginia Declaration stood as the first bill of rights attached to a written state constitution, setting a structural precedent that spread rapidly. Its rhetoric, particularly Mason's formulation that "all Men are born equally free and independant, and have certain inherent natural Rights," was directly absorbed by Jefferson into the Declaration of Independence's opening paragraphs. The document's prominence also brought into sharp relief the contradiction between its universal-rights language and the continuation of slavery in Virginia — a tension that later generations of reformers and abolitionists drew upon.

## Significance

The Virginia Declaration of Rights was the first enumerated bill of rights appended to a written constitution in American history and became the direct model for the U.S. Bill of Rights ratified in 1791. James Madison drew explicitly on the Virginia document when he introduced the constitutional amendments in Congress in 1789 that became the Bill of Rights; several provisions map directly, including Article 9's parallel to the Eighth Amendment's ban on cruel and unusual punishment. The declaration also influenced the French Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen (1789) during the French Revolution. By the end of the eighteenth century, most newly independent American states had adopted similar declarations, many borrowing from Virginia's model. Its assertion that government derives legitimacy from the consent of the governed and that individual rights precede state authority anchored the philosophical foundation of American constitutionalism.
