# Lord George Germain: Britain's War Minister and the American Strategy

**Category**: Key Figures  
**Key figures**: George Germain (Secretary of State for the Colonies), King George III, General John Burgoyne, General William Howe, Joseph Galloway

## Summary

George Germain, 1st Viscount Sackville (1716–1785), served as Secretary of State for the Colonies from November 10, 1775, until his resignation on February 9, 1782, making him Britain's chief strategist for suppressing the American Revolution throughout its critical middle years. Born George Sackville, he was the third son of the 1st Duke of Dorset. He carried the added burden of a court-martial acquittal from 1759 — found guilty of disobeying orders at the Battle of Minden (1759) during the Seven Years' War, he was publicly declared "unfit to serve His Majesty in any military capacity" by a court that included King George II himself. His appointment to lead British war policy against America was consequently controversial from the outset. As Secretary of State, Germain held responsibility for coordinating military strategy, appointing generals, provisioning forces, and liaising with the Admiralty, placing him above any single commander in the chain of strategic decision-making. Historians have labeled him "the chief architect of the American War in Britain," a designation that captures both his centrality and the widespread judgment that his miscalculations contributed directly to British defeat.

## Early Career and the Minden Controversy

Germain's pre-war career traced the contours of 18th-century British aristocratic military life:

- **1737**: Commissioned as an ensign in the British Army; rose through patronage and purchase of commissions.
- **1742–1745**: Served in the War of the Austrian Succession, distinguishing himself at Fontenoy (1745) despite the British and allied defeat.
- **1750s**: MP for Dover; cultivated political connections in the circle of William Pitt the Elder.
- **August 1, 1759**: At the **Battle of Minden** (Seven Years' War), commanding the British cavalry, he refused orders to charge retreating French forces at a decisive moment — an act of insubordination (or, his defenders argued, sound tactical caution) that cost Britain a more complete victory. Court-martialed and found guilty, he was struck from the Privy Council and effectively barred from military service.
- **1765–1775**: Rebuilt his political career as an MP and loyal supporter of Lord North's ministry. Appointed Secretary of State for the Colonies (November 10, 1775) precisely because he was a hardliner who favored military suppression of the American rebellion rather than conciliation.

His appointment was immediately attacked in Parliament: opposition leader the Marquess of Rockingham argued that placing a man condemned for military disobedience in charge of conducting the war was "a disgrace to the nation."

## Role in 1776 Campaigns

During 1776, Germain orchestrated the strategic framework within which the major British campaigns operated:

**The Southern Expedition** — Germain authorized and supported the abortive expedition against Charleston, South Carolina, that ended in the British defeat at the [Battle of Sullivan's Island](../military/battle-of-sullivans-island.md) (June 28, 1776). The failure forced the abandonment of a southern strategy for the next two years.

**The New York Campaign** — Germain coordinated the assembly and dispatch of the largest British expeditionary force to that point in history: approximately 32,000 troops under General William Howe and Admiral Richard Howe (brothers). Germain's directives shaped Howe's mandate to capture New York, suppress the rebellion in the middle colonies, and if possible negotiate a settlement from a position of overwhelming military advantage. The successful British seizure of New York in September 1776 — including the [Great Fire of New York](../military/great-fire-of-new-york-1776.md) — was the year's major military success.

**Orders to Carleton** — Germain also issued instructions to General Guy Carleton in Canada to push south, defeating the American invasion of Canada and establishing the northern force that would eventually become Burgoyne's Saratoga expedition.

## The Saratoga Failure (1776–1777)

Germain's strategic authorship of the Saratoga campaign is the central fact of his historical legacy:

- **The Plan**: Three converging columns would isolate New England: Burgoyne south from Canada via Lake Champlain; Howe north from New York; St. Leger east down the Mohawk Valley.
- **The Order**: Germain issued Howe's orders approving the Philadelphia campaign (August 1777) without explicitly requiring him to support Burgoyne — a critical failure of coordination. Multiple historians, including Piers Mackesy (*The War for America*, 1964), have debated whether Germain's orders were ambiguous or Howe acted unilaterally; the weight of evidence suggests Germain permitted Howe's southward march without recognizing it made Burgoyne's northern operation untenable.
- **The Result**: Burgoyne, unsupported, surrendered his 6,000-man army to General Horatio Gates at Saratoga on October 17, 1777. This capitulation persuaded France to sign the Treaty of Amity and Commerce with the United States (February 6, 1778) and formally enter the war — fundamentally transforming a colonial rebellion into a global war Britain could no longer prosecute cheaply.

## The Southern Strategy (1778–1781)

After Saratoga, Germain promoted the Southern Strategy, premised on his belief — reinforced by Loyalist émigré Joseph Galloway's pamphlets — that Crown sympathy was widespread in Georgia and the Carolinas. Key characteristics of Germain's conception:

1. **Loyalist mobilization**: Controlled territory would trigger mass Loyalist enlistment, enabling the British to hold the south with fewer regulars.
2. **Sequential rollup**: Georgia → South Carolina → North Carolina → Virginia, progressively strangling the rebellion from the south.
3. **Reality gap**: While British forces achieved significant successes (capture of Savannah, December 1778; fall of Charleston, May 1780), the anticipated Loyalist uprising never materialized at the scale Germain predicted. Partisan warfare (e.g., Francis Marion's operations) steadily bled British forces and collaborators.

## Personal Character and Administrative Style

Contemporary accounts and Germain's surviving correspondence reveal specific traits that shaped his strategic failures:

- **Overwork and micromanagement**: Germain ran his department with few senior staff, drafting many dispatches himself. His office generated thousands of letters to commanders, colonial governors, and naval officers — a workload that produced delays and inconsistencies.
- **Poor intelligence assessment**: He relied heavily on London-based Loyalist exiles (particularly Galloway) rather than field-derived intelligence, creating a systematically optimistic picture of colonial support for the Crown.
- **Friction with the Admiralty**: His coordination with First Lord of the Admiralty Lord Sandwich was consistently troubled. The failure to provide Burgoyne adequate naval escort on Lake Champlain was partly a product of inter-departmental rivalry.
- **Stubbornness**: Even after Saratoga, Germain resisted reconsidering fundamental assumptions, persisting with the Southern Strategy despite evidence of its limitations.

## Political Fall and Legacy

Germain resigned on February 9, 1782 — six weeks before the House of Commons passed its motion ending offensive operations in America — securing in exchange the viscountcy that made him Viscount Sackville and assured him a pension. The timing insulated him from formal parliamentary censure.

He died on August 26, 1785, at Stoneland Lodge in Sussex, having never publicly acknowledged strategic responsibility for the war's outcome. Military historians frequently cite his tenure as a case study in civilian overreach: a war minister with a compromised military reputation who substituted ideological confidence in Loyalist support for rigorous operational coordination, with consequences that permanently reshaped the British Empire.
