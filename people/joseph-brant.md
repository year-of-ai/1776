# Joseph Brant (Thayendanegea) in 1776

**Joseph Brant** (Thayendanegea, 1742–1807) was a Mohawk leader, military officer, and diplomat whose London mission of 1775–76 secured a pivotal British alliance for the Mohawk Nation, fractured the centuries-old Iroquois Confederacy, and set the trajectory for the Revolutionary War's brutal frontier theater. His 1776 actions — from the court of George III to the fields of Long Island to the Mohawk heartland — represent one of the war's most consequential Indigenous diplomatic achievements.

## Early Life and Formation

Brant was born around 1742 in the Ohio Valley during a Mohawk hunting expedition, the son of Nickus Brant (Aroghyiadecker), a Mohawk sachem. His Mohawk name, Thayendanegea, has been variously translated as "two sticks of wood bound together" — suggesting a person of united strengths.

His early formation bridged two worlds. His mother married a Mohawk leader with close British ties, and Brant grew up in the Mohawk Valley of New York alongside British colonists, developing fluent English alongside Mohawk. He attended **Moor's Indian Charity School** in Lebanon, Connecticut (founded by Eleazar Wheelock, later moved to Hanover, New Hampshire to become Dartmouth College) from around 1761, becoming one of its most accomplished students. There he mastered English grammar, Christian theology, and the written word — skills that would make him one of the most effective Indigenous negotiators in American history.

Brant's patron and brother-in-law, **Sir William Johnson**, the Superintendent of Indian Affairs for the Northern District, recognized Brant's exceptional abilities and sponsored his education and political advancement. Through Johnson's network, Brant gained access to British colonial power structures unavailable to most Indigenous leaders. When Johnson died in July 1774, his son **Sir John Johnson** and nephew **Guy Johnson** continued to leverage Brant's abilities as diplomat and military liaison.

An early scholar and translator, Brant translated the **Gospel of Mark** into the Mohawk language and helped revise the Mohawk-language Book of Common Prayer — works that illustrated both his intellectual depth and his role as a cultural bridge between Indigenous and Euro-American worlds.

## The Sister's Influence: Molly Brant

Joseph Brant's prominence owed much to his elder sister, **Mary "Molly" Brant** (c. 1736–1796), the "Mohawk Matron" who served as common-law wife and trusted adviser to Sir William Johnson until Johnson's death. Molly Brant's standing among the Iroquois Confederacy — grounded in the matrilineal social structure of Mohawk society, where women held enormous clan authority — enhanced Joseph's own legitimacy. As a clan mother, Molly wielded influence over war and peace decisions within Iroquois councils that no man could simply override. Her strong pro-British stance during the Revolutionary War shaped the Mohawk community's political alignment. When Joseph went to London in 1775, Molly maintained the pro-British network in the Mohawk Valley, working with Loyalists and transmitting intelligence to British commanders.

## London Mission (November 1775 – July 1776)

In November 1775, Brant traveled to London with **Guy Johnson**, the newly appointed Superintendent of Indian Affairs, as part of a diplomatic mission to consolidate Indigenous support for the British war effort against American rebels. The mission was carefully orchestrated: British officials needed a credible Mohawk spokesman who could speak to Crown authority directly and return to North America carrying visible proof of British commitment.

### Meetings with Crown and Government

In London, Brant secured multiple high-level audiences:

**King George III** received Brant in audience — an extraordinary distinction that few Indigenous leaders had ever achieved. Brant reportedly appeared in partial Mohawk regalia, combining elements of British formal dress with Indigenous symbols of rank, projecting both cultural confidence and diplomatic sophistication. The audience signaled formal Crown recognition of Mohawk sovereignty and partnership.

**Lord George Germain**, the Colonial Secretary who directed British strategy for the American rebellion, negotiated specific terms with Brant regarding military coordination, supply, and British commitment to Mohawk territorial interests after the war. Germain's promise that Britain would protect Mohawk lands following American defeat would later prove tragically empty (the 1783 Treaty of Paris ceded all lands east of the Mississippi without consulting Indigenous allies), but in 1776 it provided genuine inducement for Mohawk military commitment.

**Boswell and Literary London**: Brant also moved through London's intellectual circles. The famous diarist James Boswell met and interviewed Brant, publishing his impressions in the *London Magazine* in 1776 — one of the most widely-read portraits of Brant in the British public sphere. Boswell was struck by Brant's dignified intelligence and European sophistication.

### The Romney Portrait (1776)

The artist **George Romney** painted Brant's portrait during the London visit — one of the most iconic images of any Indigenous leader from the 18th century. Romney depicted Brant in partially Mohawk dress, bearing a tomahawk and wearing traditional accessories, against a neutral background emphasizing his individual presence and authority. The portrait circulated widely as an engraving, shaping British public perception of Indigenous leadership in the Revolutionary War. It now hangs in the National Gallery of Canada.

The portrait's compositional strategy — European oil-painting convention applied to an Indigenous subject presented without exoticism or condescension — itself constituted a diplomatic statement: Brant was a peer, a military ally, a figure of consequence.

### The Alliance Secured

When Brant departed London in spring 1776, he carried commitments from Germain's office: British material support (weapons, supplies, officers attached to Indigenous forces), recognition of Mohawk military authority within their territories, and formal inclusion of Mohawk in British war planning. In return, Brant would organize Mohawk and allied nations to raid American frontier settlements, disrupt rebel supply lines, and secure the northern theater against American expansion.

## Return to North America (July 1776)

Brant arrived at Staten Island in **July 1776**, disembarking into a British military establishment mobilizing for the largest amphibious operation of the Revolutionary War. His arrival coincided with the final preparations for the assault on Long Island, where over 400 British vessels were assembling to transport more than 15,000 troops.

## Battle of Long Island (August 27, 1776)

In **August 1776**, Brant participated in the **[Battle of Long Island](../military/battle-of-long-island.md)** — the opening engagement of the New York campaign and the largest battle of the entire Revolutionary War. Brant served with British forces under **General William Howe**, and according to contemporary accounts likely participated in the critical **Jamaica Pass flanking movement** that encircled Washington's Continental Army.

The Jamaica Pass maneuver — in which a 10,000-man British column marched overnight through an unguarded pass in the Heights of Guan, emerging behind American defensive lines — was the decisive tactical stroke of the battle. Washington's army was outflanked and routed; American losses exceeded 1,000 killed, captured, or missing. Brant's involvement in conventional operations alongside British regulars marked the formal integration of Indigenous warriors into the British order of battle on the eastern seaboard.

For the broader naval and amphibious context, see **[British Naval Operations in 1776](../military/british-naval-operations-1776.md)**.

## Return to Iroquois Territory (November 1776)

By **November 1776**, Brant had traveled northward to **Onoquaga** (on the Susquehanna River near present-day Windsor, New York, southeast of Binghamton), a multiethnic Indigenous settlement that served as a staging area for frontier operations. There he recruited Mohawk, Cayuga, and Seneca warriors, building the military infrastructure that would power the devastating 1777–78 frontier campaigns.

His recruitment network at Onoquaga represented a new kind of Indigenous military organization: multi-national, coordinated with British forces, and conducting extended campaigns rather than traditional raids. The **Oriskany ambush** (August 6, 1777) and the **Cherry Valley massacre** (November 11, 1778) — both major operations of the next two years — grew directly from Brant's 1776 organizational work.

## Fracturing the Iroquois Confederacy

Brant's most consequential long-term impact was the destruction of Iroquois political unity. The **Haudenosaunee Confederacy** (Six Nations: Mohawk, Onondaga, Cayuga, Seneca, Oneida, Tuscarora) had maintained collective decision-making through the Grand Council at Onondaga for centuries, with war and peace decisions requiring consensus. Brant's success in aligning the Mohawk, Cayuga, and Seneca with Britain, combined with the Oneida and Tuscarora nations' decision to support the Americans (influenced by missionary Samuel Kirkland), shattered this unity.

In the summer and fall of 1777, Iroquois fought Iroquois at **Oriskany** — one of the bloodiest battles of the Revolutionary War as a proportion of combatants engaged. The Grand Council fire at Onondaga was extinguished symbolically in 1777, marking the end of the Confederacy as a unified political entity. The 1783 Treaty of Paris, which ceded Indigenous lands without Indigenous consultation, confirmed the Confederacy's dissolution.

Brant himself remained ambivalent about this outcome. His post-war efforts focused on reconstructing an Indigenous political community in Upper Canada (present-day Ontario), where he led the Six Nations resettlement at **Grand River** (the Haldimand Tract, granted by the British in 1784) and continued arguing — unsuccessfully — that Britain owed its former allies territorial protection.

## Historical Assessment

Brant's 1776 choices were rational responses to the colonial situation as he understood it: the American rebellion threatened Indigenous sovereignty more directly than British rule, and British alliance offered the best prospect of preserving Mohawk territorial integrity. His miscalculation lay in trusting British promises; his brilliance lay in recognizing that Indigenous nations needed formal diplomatic engagement at the highest levels of European state power.

His London mission anticipated by nearly a century the diplomatic strategies that later Indigenous leaders would attempt. His multilingualism, cultural adaptability, and institutional access — products of his unusual formation across Mohawk and Anglo-American worlds — made him uniquely effective in 1776. The tragedy of his later life, watching the Grand River settlement's lands progressively encroached upon, reflected not the failure of his strategy but the failure of British reciprocity.
