# Nathan Hale: The Continental Army's Martyr Spy

**Category**: Key Figures
**Key figures**: Nathan Hale, Thomas Knowlton, George Washington, Robert Rogers, Benjamin Tallmadge

## Summary

Nathan Hale (June 6, 1755 – September 22, 1776) was a Continental Army officer executed by the British as a spy during the New York campaign of 1776, becoming one of the American Revolution's most enduring symbols of patriotic sacrifice. A Yale-educated Connecticut schoolteacher, Hale held a captain's commission in the 7th Connecticut Regiment and belonged to Knowlton's Rangers, an elite reconnaissance unit often described as the first organized American intelligence force. In September 1776, with Washington's army reeling from the loss of Long Island and desperate for information on British intentions on Manhattan, Hale volunteered for a hazardous mission behind enemy lines. He was captured within days, interrogated, and hanged without trial on the morning of September 22, 1776, at the age of twenty-one.

Hale is best remembered for the defiant last words traditionally attributed to him at the gallows—"I only regret that I have but one life to lose for my country." Although the precise wording is uncertain and rests on secondhand accounts, the sentiment cemented his place in American memory. He was designated the official state hero of Connecticut in 1985.

## Early Life and Military Service

Born in Coventry, Connecticut, into a devout farming family, Hale entered Yale College at fourteen in 1769 and graduated with honors in 1773, afterward teaching school in East Haddam and New London. At Yale he joined the Linonian Society, a debating club, and befriended classmate Benjamin Tallmadge, who would later direct Washington's Culper spy ring.

When war broke out in the spring of 1775, Hale joined a Connecticut militia company and was soon commissioned a first lieutenant in the 7th Connecticut Regiment, persuaded to full service by a letter from Tallmadge. Promoted to captain, he served through the siege of Boston and moved with the army to New York in 1776. There he was selected for Knowlton's Rangers, a reconnaissance detachment led by Lieutenant Colonel Thomas Knowlton, formed to gather intelligence during the defense of the city.

## The Spy Mission and Execution

After the British landing on Long Island and the American evacuation of Brooklyn in late August 1776, Washington urgently needed to learn where and when the British would strike next on Manhattan. When he called for a volunteer to cross into enemy-held territory, Hale accepted—reportedly the only officer to do so. Disguised as a Dutch schoolmaster seeking employment, he crossed to Long Island around September 12, 1776, to observe British troop dispositions.

The mission unraveled quickly. Accounts differ on how Hale was exposed: some hold that Major Robert Rogers, the veteran ranger commander, recognized him and lured him into revealing himself; others credit a betrayal by his Loyalist cousin Samuel Hale. Captured near Flushing Bay in Queens carrying incriminating notes, Hale was brought before General William Howe and, as an out-of-uniform spy, condemned to death without a formal trial. On the morning of September 22, 1776, he was hanged near the Park of Artillery in Manhattan.

## Significance

Nathan Hale's death gave the Revolution one of its founding martyrs. The famous farewell—preserved through the account of British engineer Captain John Montresor, who was present, and later relayed in the memoirs of Hale's fellow officer William Hull, who was not—echoed a line from Joseph Addison's popular play *Cato*, a favorite of the revolutionary generation. Historians caution that the exact words cannot be verified, but the story circulated widely and made Hale a lasting emblem of youthful devotion to the American cause.

His legacy endures in statues at Yale, City Hall Park in New York, and the CIA headquarters in Langley, Virginia, where he is honored as a forerunner of American intelligence. Numerous schools, forts, and towns bear his name, and in 1985 the Connecticut legislature formally named him the state's official hero. His counterpart in British service, Major John André—hanged as a spy by the Americans in 1780—would later invite direct comparison, underscoring the perilous, unprotected status of intelligence officers on both sides of the war.
