# Benjamin Franklin & 1776 Diplomacy

**Category**: Key Figures
**Key figures**: Benjamin Franklin, Silas Deane, Arthur Lee, Charles Gravier (Comte de Vergennes), Pierre-Augustin Caron de Beaumarchais, John Adams, Thomas Jefferson

## Summary

Benjamin Franklin, then 70 years old, served in 1776 as the American Revolution's most consequential diplomat. Over the course of the year he participated in four distinct critical roles: member of the Committee of Secret Correspondence (the proto-State Department), delegate to the failed Canada mission, member of the Committee of Five that drafted the Declaration of Independence, and — as the year's most significant contribution — leader of the American diplomatic commission dispatched to France in October 1776. His fame as a natural philosopher and his deep European contacts made him the only American whose personal reputation could command the attention of the French court.

### Committee of Secret Correspondence

The Second Continental Congress established the Committee of Secret Correspondence on November 29, 1775, to maintain clandestine communication with potential European allies. Its original members were Benjamin Franklin, John Dickinson, Benjamin Harrison, John Jay, and Thomas Johnson, with Robert Morris joining shortly after. Franklin was its most active member, leveraging contacts built during his two decades as colonial agent in London and his scientific correspondence with French philosophes. The committee's first European agent was Arthur Lee in London; in December 1775 it met clandestinely at Carpenters' Hall in Philadelphia with Julien Alexandre Achard de Bonvouloir, an unofficial French agent, to sound out France's willingness to provide covert assistance. Based on these overtures and Franklin's dispatches, French Foreign Minister Charles Gravier, Comte de Vergennes, authorized the creation of a fictitious commercial house — Roderigue Hortalez & Cie — under playwright and arms dealer Pierre-Augustin Caron de Beaumarchais. Funded with one million livres each from France and Spain in May 1776, the company channeled muskets, cannon, gunpowder, clothing for 30,000 men, and other military supplies to the Continental Army, shipments that proved critical to the Saratoga campaign of 1777.

In March 1776, the committee dispatched Silas Deane to Paris with instructions (drafted largely by Franklin) to approach Vergennes with two requests: a loan of arms and military stores, and assurance that France would recognize American independence if declared. Deane arrived in May 1776 and began secret negotiations that ran in parallel with the Hortalez & Cie arrangements.

### Canada Mission: February–May 1776

On February 15, 1776, Congress appointed a three-person commission — Franklin, Samuel Chase, and Charles Carroll of Carrollton — to travel to Montreal and persuade Canadian colonists to join the American cause or at minimum remain neutral. The mission was a severe physical ordeal for the 70-year-old Franklin, requiring a winter journey through wilderness conditions north of New York. The commission arrived in Montreal in late April but found that the Continental Army's chaotic occupation, its seizure of goods without payment, and its antagonism toward the Catholic Church had thoroughly alienated the local population. Franklin, weakened by the journey and suffering from gout, returned to Philadelphia by late May, reporting to Congress that Canada could not be won by persuasion. The failure solidified his view that French military alliance, not Canadian accession, was the necessary strategic priority.

### Committee of Five and the Declaration of Independence

On June 11, 1776, Congress appointed Franklin to the Committee of Five alongside Thomas Jefferson, John Adams, Roger Sherman, and Robert R. Livingston to draft a declaration of independence. Franklin's advanced age and poor health led to Jefferson becoming the primary drafter. Franklin made several editorial changes to Jefferson's draft, the most celebrated of which may have been altering "sacred and undeniable" truths to "self-evident truths" — though historians debate the precise attribution of this change. The Declaration was adopted by Congress on July 4, 1776. Franklin was one of its 56 signatories when it was formally engrossed and signed on August 2, 1776.

### Peace Conference and Diplomatic Commission

Following the British victory at the [Battle of Long Island](../military/battle-of-long-island.md) (August 27, 1776), British Admiral Lord Richard Howe paroled General John Sullivan and sent him to Congress with an offer of a peace conference. Franklin, John Adams, and Edward Rutledge met with Admiral Howe on Staten Island on September 11, 1776. Howe offered reconciliation under conditions requiring revocation of the Declaration of Independence. The American commissioners refused, and the conference ended without result — but it confirmed to London that the colonies were committed to full independence. On September 26, 1776, Congress appointed Franklin, Silas Deane, and Thomas Jefferson (Jefferson declined; Arthur Lee took the post) as Commissioners to negotiate a Treaty of Alliance and Treaty of Amity and Commerce with France.

### Public Diplomacy and the Paris Press

Franklin understood from his earliest weeks in France that his personal celebrity was a diplomatic instrument as potent as any formal credential. He cultivated French pamphleteers, philosophes, and journalists to sustain favorable coverage of the American cause in the Paris press. His letters and essays — circulated through Parisian salons and reproduced in French periodicals — framed the American Revolution as a vindication of Enlightenment principles: natural rights, commerce free from mercantilist restriction, and governance by consent. The contrast between his plain Quaker-style dress and the powdered-wig formality of Versailles was not accidental; Franklin carefully maintained his image as a republican sage, printing portraits and medallions of himself that sold briskly across Paris.

The Masonic lodges of Paris provided an additional channel of informal influence outside formal diplomatic protocol. Franklin was proposed for membership in the Lodge of the Nine Sisters (Loge des Neuf Sœurs) — whose members included the encyclopédistes Condorcet and Voltaire — and was formally admitted in April 1778. His Masonic networks in the months following his December 1776 arrival supplemented the official contacts managed through Vergennes. French public enthusiasm generated by this sustained press and social campaign created popular and parliamentary pressure that made indefinite denial of open alliance diplomatically untenable for the French crown, even before the Saratoga news arrived in December 1777.

The French financial commitment to the covert war — the million-livre contributions from France and Spain that funded [Roderigue Hortalez & Cie](../economics/continental-congress-finance-1776.md) — was partly a product of this same diplomatic pressure. Franklin's cultivation of Vergennes and the wider French court was inseparable from the fiscal arrangements that kept the Continental Army in the field.

### Departure and Arrival in France

On October 26, 1776, Franklin sailed from Philadelphia aboard the sloop-of-war *Reprisal*, accompanied by his two grandsons — William Temple Franklin (who would serve as his secretary) and Benjamin Franklin Bache. After a stormy Atlantic crossing, the party landed at Auray in Brittany on December 3, 1776, and traveled overland to Paris, arriving at the village of Passy (now part of Paris) where Franklin established his residence at the Hôtel de Valentinois, offered free of charge by a sympathetic French merchant. On December 28, 1776, Franklin had his first formal meeting with Vergennes, presenting the American commissioners' case for a formal French alliance. Vergennes, though sympathetic, demurred pending clearer evidence of American military viability — evidence that would arrive in December 1777 with news of the British defeat at Saratoga.

Despite the inconclusive diplomatic opening, Franklin's presence in Paris produced an immediate popular effect. His simple dress, fur cap, and bifocal spectacles contrasted theatrically with the powdered wigs and court finery of Versailles, and French society received him as a living embodiment of Enlightenment virtue — scientist, statesman, and republican philosopher in one figure. This celebrity status proved diplomatically useful, generating public pressure on the French government to support the American cause and frustrating British efforts to suppress French aid.

## Significance

Franklin's work in 1776 established the diplomatic and covert supply networks that sustained the Continental Army through its most difficult years. The Hortalez & Cie supply operation, developed in parallel with his committee correspondence, delivered materiel that historians credit as essential to the survival of the American cause in 1776–1777. His personal reputation in France created a political climate that made the Treaty of Alliance of February 6, 1778 — providing open French military, naval, and financial support — achievable. No other American figure possessed the combination of scientific fame, European social connections, and diplomatic dexterity that the mission required. His appointment as Minister to France in 1778 and his negotiation of the Treaty of Paris (1783) both flowed directly from the groundwork he laid in the closing months of 1776.
