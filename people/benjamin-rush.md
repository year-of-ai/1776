# Benjamin Rush

**Category**: Key Figures  
**Key figures**: Benjamin Rush, Thomas Paine, George Washington, Richard Stockton, John Adams

## Summary

Benjamin Rush (1746–1813) was a physician, educator, and political leader who became one of the 56 signers of the Declaration of Independence. After earning his medical degree from the University of Edinburgh in 1768, Rush established himself as a chemist and educator at the College of Philadelphia, where he served as Professor of Chemistry. In 1776, Rush played a dual role as both a Continental Congress delegate representing Pennsylvania and a pioneering military physician. He collaborated closely with Thomas Paine on *Common Sense*, the era's most influential pro-independence pamphlet, and was instrumental in bringing that work to publication. By December 1776, Rush had begun his medical service with the Continental Army, treating wounded soldiers including those from the Battle of Trenton, establishing the framework for organized military medicine in America.

## Early Life and Medical Education

Benjamin Rush was born on January 4, 1746 (December 24, 1745, Old Style), in Byberry Township (now part of Philadelphia), Pennsylvania, to John Rush and Susanna Hall Harvey. His father died when Benjamin was five, and his mother ran a grocery store to support the family. Rush's intellectual promise attracted the attention of Reverend Samuel Finley, his uncle by marriage, who educated him at the West Nottingham Academy in Maryland. Rush entered the College of New Jersey (now Princeton University) in 1759 at age 13, graduating in 1760 at just 14 years old.

Rush studied medicine under Dr. John Redman in Philadelphia from 1761 to 1766, then sailed to Edinburgh to attend one of the world's leading medical schools. He graduated from the University of Edinburgh in 1768, having written his thesis on the digestion of food in the stomach. During his Edinburgh years he encountered the leading lights of the Scottish Enlightenment, attending lectures by William Cullen and corresponding with Benjamin Franklin, then serving as colonial agent in London. Rush spent additional months in London and Paris before returning to Philadelphia in 1769.

Upon his return he was immediately appointed **Professor of Chemistry** at the College of Philadelphia — the first chemistry professorship in America — and began private medical practice. His 1770 textbook *Syllabus of a Course of Lectures on Chemistry* was the first such text published in North America, marking him as a pioneer of American scientific education. Rush was 24 years old.

## Role in *Common Sense* and Revolutionary Politics

Rush's involvement in the publication of [Thomas Paine's *Common Sense*](thomas-paine.md) was decisive for the pamphlet's impact. In late 1775, Rush had been contemplating writing a similar argument for independence himself but concluded that a piece so radical would destroy his medical practice and political standing. He recognized that Paine — a newly arrived English émigré with no professional reputation to protect — was the ideal author.

Rush met Paine in late 1775, shared his ideas for the pamphlet's structure and main arguments, and introduced Paine to the Philadelphia printer Robert Bell. Rush also suggested the title *Common Sense*, which Paine adopted. He reviewed early drafts and helped revise the pamphlet's opening sections. *Common Sense* was published on January 10, 1776, and sold approximately 100,000 copies within three months, an extraordinary circulation for the era. The pamphlet's argument — that monarchy was inherently corrupt and that American independence was not merely defensible but morally necessary — shifted colonial opinion dramatically toward independence over the spring and summer of 1776 (see [Declaration of Independence](../politics/declaration-of-independence.md)).

Rush was elected to the Continental Congress as a Pennsylvania delegate on July 20, 1776 — just sixteen days after the Declaration's adoption — replacing a delegate who had stepped down. He signed the engrossed copy of the Declaration in August 1776, joining fellow Pennsylvanian and relative by marriage **Richard Stockton** (who was later captured by the British and became the only signer to recant under duress, though he later reaffirmed his support for independence).

At Congress, Rush served on committees overseeing hospital arrangements and military medicine, reflecting his primary contribution: not as a legislative politician but as a scientific administrator who could bridge medicine and military logistics.

## Military Medicine and the 1776 Campaign

Rush's most consequential 1776 work was establishing the organizational framework for military medicine. He joined the Continental Army's medical service in the closing months of 1776, during the catastrophic retreat across New Jersey and the subsequent counteroffensives at Trenton and Princeton.

When Rush arrived at the Army in December 1776, the medical situation was desperate. Hospitals were overcrowded, poorly supplied, and rife with typhus, dysentery, and smallpox. Surgeons frequently lacked basic instruments; wound dressings were made from whatever cloth could be found. Rush was appalled and immediately began lobbying Washington and Congress for systematic reform.

On December 26, 1776, Washington's army crossed the Delaware and won the surprise [Battle of Trenton](../military/battle-of-trenton.md). Rush was present as a physician treating the wounded on both sides, including Hessian prisoners. His firsthand observation of battlefield wounds, surgical outcomes, and the relative effectiveness of different treatments generated data he would use in his later writings on military medicine. He noted in his journal that the Hessian wounded received inferior care because American surgeons were assigned to American soldiers first — a triage hierarchy he argued was medically and morally unjustifiable.

Rush's 1777 pamphlet *Directions for Preserving the Health of Soldiers* grew directly from his 1776 field observations. He argued that disease — not enemy fire — was the primary killer of soldiers, and that hygiene, ventilation, diet, and sanitation could save more lives than any surgical intervention. The pamphlet recommended specific measures: regular bathing, dry encampments, fresh food when possible, and the treatment of fever by fresh air rather than the common practice of "sweating" patients in sealed rooms. The Continental Army eventually adopted many of Rush's recommendations, and the pamphlet remained a standard reference for American military medicine into the 19th century.

## Conflict with Washington and the Conway Cabal

Rush's relationship with [George Washington](george-washington-1776.md) became deeply contentious by late 1777. Rush grew convinced that Washington was a poor commander whose timidity at the Battle of White Plains and failure to defend Fort Washington had needlessly prolonged the war. He wrote an anonymous letter to Governor Patrick Henry of Virginia in December 1777 criticizing Washington's leadership and implying that Generals Gates and Conway were more capable commanders — a letter connected to what became known as the "Conway Cabal," a supposed conspiracy to replace Washington as commander-in-chief.

The letter was eventually identified as Rush's, permanently damaging his relationship with Washington and his standing in Congress. Rush resigned his military post in January 1778. Though the "Conway Cabal" proved to be far less organized than initially feared, the episode illustrated the intense political pressures surrounding military command in 1777–1778, as the Continental Army struggled to maintain cohesion after the losses at Brandywine and Germantown.

Despite the falling out with Washington, Rush's criticisms of military hospital conditions led to a comprehensive reform of the Continental Army's medical department in 1780, vindicating many of his recommendations.

## Intellectual Contributions and Scientific Legacy

Rush's 1776 activities cannot be separated from his broader intellectual project. He was simultaneously a medical practitioner, a political theorist, and a moral reformer. His 1773 pamphlet *An Address to the Inhabitants of the British Settlements in America upon Slave-Keeping* was one of the first anti-slavery publications by a prominent colonial American, and he was a founding member of the Pennsylvania Society for Promoting the Abolition of Slavery (established 1775).

During the 1776 period Rush was engaged with the [Scottish Enlightenment](../arts/scottish-enlightenment-1776.md) intellectual tradition he had absorbed in Edinburgh — particularly the moral philosophy of Francis Hutcheson and the social medicine of William Cullen. He applied these frameworks to American political thought, arguing that good government, like good medicine, required understanding human nature scientifically rather than theologically.

Rush's later career (1780s–1813) built on foundations laid in 1776: his work on mental illness at the Pennsylvania Hospital would earn him the title "Father of American Psychiatry"; his *Medical Inquiries and Observations upon the Diseases of the Mind* (1812) remained the standard American psychiatric text for 70 years. His 1805 revival of the friendship between John Adams and Thomas Jefferson — Rush encouraged both aging statesmen to reconcile — is one of the most celebrated episodes in American intellectual history.

## Character and Contemporaries

John Adams described Rush as "too much of a talker to be a deep thinker" but acknowledged him as "an excellent man" and indispensable colleague. Adams valued Rush's combination of medical authority and political engagement and corresponded with him throughout their long friendship. Rush in turn considered Adams "the Atlas of American independence" for his role in steering the Continental Congress toward the July 2, 1776 vote (see [Lee Resolution](../politics/lee-resolution.md)).

Rush's circle in 1776 encompassed the full range of revolutionary leadership: he knew Benjamin Franklin from his student years, corresponded with John Adams about the nature of republican government, collaborated with Thomas Paine on *Common Sense*, signed the Declaration alongside colleagues including John Witherspoon (whose Scottish Enlightenment connections Rush shared), and treated soldiers under George Washington's command.

Unlike many of the Declaration's signers, Rush paid a direct personal price: British forces occupied his Philadelphia home and laboratory in 1777, destroying equipment and medical records. He evacuated his family to Princeton, New Jersey, where he continued medical practice while Congress met in exile.

## Death and Memorialization

Rush died of typhus in Philadelphia on April 19, 1813, at age 67. He was buried at Christ Church Burial Ground in Philadelphia, where [Benjamin Franklin](benjamin-franklin-1776.md) and four other Declaration signers also rest. The Benjamin Rush Memorial Scholarship at the University of Pennsylvania's Perelman School of Medicine perpetuates his name, as does the U.S. Army's Benjamin Rush Institute of Behavioral Science. His portrait appears in the collection of the National Portrait Gallery, Smithsonian Institution.

His life embodies the Enlightenment ideal that scientific knowledge and civic virtue were inseparable — that understanding the body and reforming society were parts of the same moral project.
