## Summary

The Battle of Trenton was fought on the morning of December 26, 1776, in Trenton, New Jersey. It was preceded by one of the most celebrated operations of the American Revolutionary War: George Washington's crossing of the Delaware River on the night of December 25–26. Approximately 2,400 Continental Army soldiers and 18 artillery pieces crossed the icy river during a severe winter storm, guided by Colonel Henry Knox who managed the logistics of the passage.

Trenton was garrisoned by three Hessian regiments under Colonel Johann Rall, totaling approximately 1,400 men. Rall, a veteran commander with thirty-six years of Hessian military experience, had received warnings of a possible attack but dismissed them, reportedly saying "Let them come." He had not ordered defensive fortifications constructed around the town. Washington divided his force into two columns — one under his own command approaching from the north along the Pennington Road, and one under General Nathanael Greene from the northwest — to converge simultaneously on the town. The attack commenced at approximately 8:00 a.m. American artillery commanded the main streets before Hessian resistance could be effectively organized. The fighting lasted roughly forty-five minutes. Colonel Rall was mortally wounded while attempting to rally his troops and formally surrendered to Washington before dying later that day.

American forces suffered approximately 5 killed and 4 wounded in the fighting itself. The Hessian garrison suffered 22 killed, 83 wounded, and approximately 900 captured, along with 6 field artillery pieces seized. Washington's forces withdrew back across the Delaware with their prisoners and captured equipment on the evening of December 26.

## Significance

The Battle of Trenton was Washington's first major offensive victory of the Revolutionary War, coming after a succession of defeats in 1776 — including the loss of New York City, the Battle of Long Island, and the retreat through New Jersey — that had severely depleted Continental Army strength and morale. The victory demonstrated that British-allied professional forces were vulnerable to unconventional tactics and winter operations, directly undermining the perception of Hessian and British invincibility. News of the outcome spread rapidly through the colonies, encouraging militia enlistments and renewed public support for the revolutionary cause. The operation was immediately followed by a second victory at the Battle of Princeton on January 3, 1777. Together the two engagements shifted the strategic balance in New Jersey and secured the Continental Army's winter encampment. Historians regard the Trenton-Princeton campaign as a turning point that preserved the Continental Army as a fighting force at a moment when the Revolution was in serious danger of collapse.
