## Summary

The Battle of Valcour Island, also called the Battle of Valcour Bay, was a naval engagement fought on October 11, 1776, on Lake Champlain, with a running pursuit continuing through October 13. It pitted a small American squadron under Brigadier General Benedict Arnold against a larger British flotilla directed by the governor of Quebec, General Guy Carleton, and commanded afloat by Captain Thomas Pringle. The action arose from the failed American invasion of Canada: as the Continental forces fell back up the lake, both sides raced through the summer of 1776 to build fleets that would control Lake Champlain, the invasion corridor between Canada and the upper Hudson valley.

Arnold's fleet — roughly sixteen vessels — flat-bottomed gundalows and larger row galleys, most built at Skenesborough and including the schooner *Royal Savage* and the galley *Congress* and gundalow *Philadelphia* — was outmatched by the British, who had assembled superior firepower topped by the 18-gun ship *Inflexible*, the schooners *Maria* and *Carleton*, a heavy radeau, and a large number of gunboats. Recognizing his inferiority in guns and seamanship, Arnold anchored his line in the narrow strait between Valcour Island and the New York shore, where the British — sailing down the lake before a north wind — would have to beat back upwind against him and could not bring their full weight to bear at once.

The British passed the island before sighting the American line and had to work back against the wind to engage. Heavy close-range gunfire began around midday; the *Royal Savage* ran aground and was lost early, and by evening most of Arnold's boats were battered, the *Philadelphia* sinking soon after nightfall. With his squadron crippled and low on ammunition, Arnold slipped his fleet through a gap in the British cordon during a dark, foggy night and rowed south toward Crown Point. The British discovered the escape the next morning and pursued; in a running fight on October 13 they captured or destroyed several more vessels, and Arnold deliberately ran his remaining boats aground and burned them rather than surrender. American losses came to roughly eleven of the sixteen vessels and about eighty men killed or wounded with over a hundred captured, against some forty British casualties.

## Significance

- **A tactical defeat that bought decisive time**: Though the British destroyed most of Arnold's fleet and won the day, the two seasons of shipbuilding the Americans forced on Carleton, together with the losses and the lateness of the year, proved strategically decisive. Carleton reoccupied Crown Point but, facing the fortified garrison at Fort Ticonderoga and the onset of winter, chose to withdraw north to Canada for the season rather than press an assault.
- **Ticonderoga and the northern front preserved**: The delay meant the British did not advance down the Hudson corridor in 1776. That reprieve gave the Continental Army the winter and following year to prepare, setting the stage for the 1777 campaign in which a renewed British thrust from Canada ended in the surrender at Saratoga.
- **A foundational moment for American naval warfare**: Valcour Island is often cited as one of the first fleet actions of the United States Navy's lineage, and Arnold's improvised construction and aggressive defense became a celebrated example of trading tactical loss for strategic advantage.
