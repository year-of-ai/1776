## Summary

The Second Continental Congress in 1776 faced an immediate and structural fiscal crisis: it possessed no power to levy taxes under the Articles of Confederation framework then being drafted, no established credit with foreign lenders, and no central treasury. Yet it was waging a war against the largest military power in the Western world. The three instruments it deployed — paper currency emission, domestic loan offices, and covert foreign borrowing — each carried severe limitations that produced inflationary pressures by the year's end and a fiscal fragility that haunted the republic for a decade. The Continental Congress's financial predicament in 1776 stands in striking contrast to the free-market framework articulated that same year in Adam Smith's *The Wealth of Nations*, which critiqued both mercantilist state regulation and the distortions produced by debased currency.

### The Constitutional Constraint

The Continental Congress inherited the colonists' deep aversion to direct taxation — one of the central grievances of the Revolutionary movement. The draft Articles of Confederation, circulated in 1776, denied Congress any power of direct taxation, leaving revenue requisition from the thirteen states as the only constitutional mechanism. In practice, the states remitted little or nothing during the war years; they were consumed by their own military expenses and faced the same scarcity of hard money. Congress was thus forced to finance the war almost entirely without tax revenue.

### Continental Currency: Emission and Early Depreciation

The Continental Congress had first authorized paper currency — "bills of credit" — in June 1775, issuing $2 million in Continental dollars structured as zero-coupon instruments redeemable at face value in future years. A further $4 million emission was authorized on February 17, 1776. By November 1776, total Continental currency in circulation reached approximately $25 million, already trading at a 30 percent discount relative to hard silver (specie). Because Congress lacked taxing power, there was no credible mechanism by which the currency would be redeemed; holders understood this, and confidence deteriorated steadily. The phrase "not worth a Continental" entered popular usage during and after the war to describe anything of negligible value.

The currency's depreciation was not merely a financial inconvenience: it constituted a regressive tax falling most heavily on soldiers paid in paper, on artisans and farmers who accepted it for goods, and on the poor who lacked the assets to hedge against inflation. Continental soldiers' effective wages declined in real terms throughout the war, contributing to desertion, mutiny, and the near-collapse of the army at Valley Forge in 1777–1778. By November 1778, $6 in paper was required to purchase $1 worth of goods; by November 1779, $40 was required. The final collapse came in 1780 when Congress attempted a revaluation scheme demanding states raise taxes eighty times beyond historical levels, destroying what remained of public confidence.

### Domestic Loan Offices

In 1776, Congress established Loan Offices in each of the thirteen states to receive subscriptions to domestic loans in exchange for interest-bearing certificates (Loan Office certificates) paying approximately 4 to 6 percent annually. These certificates were intended to draw private savings into the war effort and to provide an alternative to pure currency emission. In practice, the loan offices raised modest sums compared to the war's costs; most private capital holders — where it existed — preferred to hold land, goods, or hard money rather than paper obligations of a government whose survival was uncertain. The loan office system was an important institutional innovation, anticipating the later bond financing used by the United States Treasury, but it could not close the fiscal gap in 1776.

### Military Expenditure and Cash Deficits in 1776

Congressional pay orders and supply requisitions in 1776 were staggering relative to any prior American experience. The Continental Army's estimated annual operating cost approached $20 million (Continental) by late 1776, covering soldiers' pay, rations, clothing, artillery, and the vast logistical demands of a field army. The **New York and New Jersey campaigns** from August through December 1776 — beginning with the [Battle of Long Island]({{ '/news/military/battle-of-long-island/' | relative_url }}) (August 27), continuing through the loss of Manhattan (September 15), and culminating in Washington's retreat across New Jersey — generated acute, immediate cash demands that overwhelmed every available revenue mechanism simultaneously. Congress authorized an additional $5 million Continental dollar emission on November 2, 1776, and further emissions in December, pushing total paper currency outstanding toward $25 million by year-end. These wartime demands made clear that the gap between Congressional expenditure and sustainable revenue was structural: without foreign subsidies or a constitutional reform granting Congress taxing power, the army could not survive another campaign season.

### Foreign Borrowing and Covert French Aid

The most consequential financial development of 1776 was the opening of covert foreign assistance from France and Spain. The Committee of Secret Correspondence, with [Benjamin Franklin]({{ '/news/people/benjamin-franklin-1776/' | relative_url }}) as its most active member, had cultivated contacts with French officials through 1775–1776. In May 1776, the French government secretly provided one million livres and Spain provided a matching million to fund Roderigue Hortalez & Cie, a fictitious trading company organized by playwright-arms dealer Pierre-Augustin Caron de Beaumarchais. The company procured and shipped muskets, artillery, gunpowder, tents, and uniforms — reportedly sufficient to equip 30,000 men — to American forces. These supplies were delivered without any formal financial obligation recorded between the governments, providing France with plausible deniability. They arrived in time to equip Continental units that fought at Saratoga in October 1777, whose victory catalyzed the formal French alliance of February 1778.

Prior to open alliance, France secretly advanced approximately 2 million livres tournois through 1777 to help Congress meet interest payments on its Loan Office certificates — paid via bills of exchange drawn on American credit in France. This mechanism allowed Congress to maintain some appearance of creditworthiness with domestic lenders while the war continued.

### Contrast with *The Wealth of Nations*

Adam Smith's *The Wealth of Nations*, published March 9, 1776 — the same year the Continental Congress was desperately improvising its fiscal machinery — offered a direct analytical framework for understanding the colonists' predicament. Smith's Book IV dismantled the mercantilist system that British commercial policy had enforced on the colonies, arguing that trade restrictions, monopoly grants, and controlled currency were economically destructive. Smith's Book V analyzed the proper sources of government revenue — chiefly taxation — and warned explicitly against the distortions caused by paper money not backed by specie. The Continental Congress in 1776 was, in effect, a case study in the fiscal pathologies Smith described: a government unable to tax, forced into currency inflation, dependent on foreign subsidies, and producing the commercial dislocations that Smith's free-market theory predicted. Several founders, including Thomas Jefferson and James Madison, read Smith, though the immediate wartime exigencies left little room to implement his prescriptions during the conflict.

### Institutional Legacy

The fiscal crisis of 1776–1783 was never fully resolved during the Revolutionary War. Congress appointed Robert Morris as Superintendent of Finance in 1781, granting him unprecedented executive authority over the country's accounts; Morris used his personal credit and mercantile connections to keep the army in the field through the Yorktown campaign. The experience shaped the Constitutional Convention of 1787 profoundly: the framers granted Congress explicit powers to levy taxes, borrow money, and regulate currency, and prohibited the states from emitting bills of credit — a direct constitutional response to the Continental currency disaster. In 1790, Secretary of the Treasury Alexander Hamilton's First Report on Public Credit proposed federal assumption of state war debts and redemption of Continental debt at face value, providing the institutional foundation for American public finance that the Continental Congress had never possessed.

## Significance

The fiscal arrangements of 1776 were among the most consequential — and most precarious — features of the American Revolution. The Continental Congress's improvised finance kept an army in the field against a superpower for eight years, but at the cost of ruinous inflation, eroded credibility, and persistent military shortfalls. The crisis demonstrated that political independence required fiscal institutions capable of sustaining it, a lesson that drove the Constitutional Convention's grant of taxing and borrowing powers to the federal government. The events of 1776 — simultaneous with *The Wealth of Nations*' publication and the opening of covert French assistance — illustrate the intersection of revolutionary politics, economic theory, and great-power diplomacy that defined the founding moment of the United States.
