## Summary

The Declaration of Independence, adopted by the Second Continental Congress on July 4, 1776, was first publicly proclaimed on July 8, 1776, simultaneously in Philadelphia, Trenton (New Jersey), and Easton (Pennsylvania). The announcement was orchestrated primarily through public readings before assembled crowds, with copies distributed via the *Dunlap broadside* — a single-sheet printing ordered by Congress on the night of July 4 and typeset overnight by Philadelphia printer John Dunlap. Colonel John Nixon of the Pennsylvania Associators delivered the first recorded public reading from the State House yard (Independence Hall) in Philadelphia at approximately 12:00 p.m. on July 8. The following day, July 9, the Declaration was read to Washington's Continental Army troops in New York City, who afterward toppled the gilded equestrian statue of King George III on the Bowling Green — its lead later melted down into a reported 42,088 musket balls. The Liberty Bell's role on July 8 as a crowd-summoning instrument is supported by contemporary newspaper accounts, though the specific bell rung has been debated by 20th-century historians.

## The Dunlap Broadside

The primary physical instrument of proclamation was the *Dunlap broadside*, the first printed text of the Declaration:

- **Night of July 4, 1776**: Congress ordered the Declaration engrossed and "authenticated," directing Charles Thomson (Secretary of Congress) and John Hancock (President) to sign a printed copy for distribution. The Committee of Five (Jefferson, Adams, Franklin, Livingston, Sherman) had finished the final text that afternoon.
- **Overnight, July 4–5**: Scottish immigrant printer John Dunlap typeset and printed the broadside at his shop at 48 High Street (now Market Street), Philadelphia. His pressrun is estimated at 150–200 copies, though the exact number is unknown.
- **July 5–6**: Copies were dispatched by rider to the thirteen colonial governments and to General Washington's headquarters in New York. Congress retained copies for official business.
- **Surviving copies**: As of 2024, 26 authenticated Dunlap broadsides survive. One sold at auction in 2000 for $8.1 million — the highest price paid for any historical document at that time.

The parchment engrossed copy (the document usually called "the Declaration") was not completed until August 2, 1776, when most delegates signed it; this is the document now displayed at the National Archives in Washington, D.C.

## Sequence of Proclamations, July 8, 1776

### Philadelphia (July 8, ~12:00 p.m.)
The Pennsylvania Packet and Daily Advertiser reported that a "great crowd" assembled in the State House yard after the Liberty Bell (then called the State House Bell) was rung. Colonel John Nixon, a wealthy Philadelphia merchant and militia officer, read the text aloud. Contemporary accounts describe the crowd responding with "three huzzahs" and celebrating through the evening with bonfires and the ringing of city church bells.

### Trenton, New Jersey (July 8)
The New Jersey Committee of Safety ordered a reading in Trenton the same day; local militia officers conducted the public reading for the colonial capital's residents.

### Easton, Pennsylvania (July 8)
A public reading was conducted at the Easton courthouse steps before crowds from the surrounding county.

### New York City (July 9, evening)
Washington ordered the Declaration read to "the several brigades" of the Continental Army. An officer read it before assembled troops at 6:00 p.m. Following the reading, soldiers and civilians — reportedly joined by members of the Sons of Liberty — marched to the Bowling Green and toppled the lead equestrian statue of George III (erected 1770). The statue's 4,000 pounds of lead were transported to Litchfield, Connecticut, where Loyalist women under the direction of Oliver Wolcott (a Connecticut delegate who signed the Declaration) cast the metal into musket balls: reported as 42,088 rounds.

### Further Distribution
By late July and August 1776, the Declaration had been read publicly in town squares from Massachusetts to Georgia, with each colonial assembly or local committee organizing its own proclamation ceremony. German-language versions appeared in Pennsylvania newspapers by July 9, reaching the colony's substantial German-speaking population.

## The Liberty Bell Debate

The Liberty Bell's association with July 8, 1776 rests primarily on a July 8 entry in the diary of watchman Christopher Marshall, who recorded church bells ringing throughout the day. A 1847 short story, "Ring, Grandfather, Ring" by Philadelphia writer George Lippard, popularized the specific image of an aged bellman waiting to ring the State House bell upon receiving news of the vote — a story acknowledged as fictional but widely repeated. Historians confirm the State House bell was rung on July 8 as a crowd-summoning device, consistent with standard 18th-century civic practice. The Liberty Bell's specific crack is generally dated to the 1840s (it cracked irreparably while being rung for George Washington's birthday in 1846), not to 1776.

## Philosophical Framing in the Proclamation Context

The Declaration's effectiveness as a proclamation rested on its structure: it combined a philosophical preamble accessible to literate and illiterate listeners alike, a list of concrete grievances against George III that audiences could recognize from personal experience, and a formal assertion of independence signed by identifiable representatives. Key rhetorical elements that functioned distinctively when read aloud:

- **Anaphoric structure of grievances**: "He has refused…He has forbidden…He has endeavored…" created a rhetorically cumulative effect optimal for public oral delivery.
- **Universal claims**: "All men are created equal" and "unalienable Rights" positioned the American cause within a natural-rights framework intelligible to audiences familiar with John Locke's *Second Treatise* (1689) and Thomas Paine's *Common Sense* (January 1776).
- **Named signatories**: The public reading of John Hancock's name (the only name on the Dunlap broadside) added personal political accountability — these were identifiable colonists staking their lives.

Thomas Paine's *Common Sense*, published January 10, 1776, had primed colonial audiences for independence arguments over the preceding six months, making the Declaration's proclamation fall on prepared ground. An estimated 100,000–150,000 copies of *Common Sense* had circulated by July, reaching perhaps half the colonial adult population.

## International Dimensions

The public proclamation was diplomatically calculated as well as domestically oriented:

- **French response**: French Foreign Minister Vergennes received the Declaration's text through diplomatic channels by August 1776. French interest in fomenting British difficulties had been established through Beaumarchais's supply network (*Rodrigue Hortalez et Cie*) since May 1776, but the formal Declaration's public commitment to independence strengthened the case for official French recognition.
- **Dutch reception**: Dutch merchants and politicians — already covertly supplying the Continental Army through the island of St. Eustatius — regarded the Declaration's formal assertion of sovereignty as moving the Americans closer to the status of a legitimate trading partner rather than British rebels.
- **German states**: Several German newspapers reprinted the Declaration in translation during August–September 1776, framing it in Enlightenment terms; this created irony given that 30,000 Hessian mercenaries were simultaneously crossing the Atlantic to fight against the Americans.

## Significance

The public proclamation of the Declaration served simultaneously as a political act (formalizing the break from Britain), a psychological event (galvanizing colonial commitment to independence), a diplomatic signal (communicating American political seriousness to European powers), and a propaganda campaign (articulating the moral foundation of the Revolution). Its success as a proclamation was inseparable from its structure as a document: Jefferson's text was crafted to be read aloud, argued publicly, and reprinted in newspapers. The practice of simultaneous readings in multiple cities constituted one of the first large-scale public information campaigns in American history, exploiting the colonial newspaper network established over the preceding decades.
