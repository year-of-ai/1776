## Summary

Button Gwinnett (1735–1777) was born in Gloucester, England, and emigrated to America in 1762 as a merchant. By 1773, he had settled in St. Catherine's Parish, Georgia, acquiring land and enslaved workers to establish himself as a planter. In 1775, he was elected to Georgia's Provincial Congress, a position that placed him at the center of the state's reluctant path to independence. Georgia was the most southern and weakly settled of the thirteen colonies, with significant Loyalist sentiment and limited direct conflict until 1776–1777. Gwinnett emerged as a moderate voice initially, then as a proponent of independence by mid-1776.

In August 1776, Gwinnett signed the engrossed Declaration of Independence as one of Georgia's three delegates (alongside Lyman Hall and George Walton). His signature committed him irreversibly to the Revolutionary cause, yet his subsequent leadership of Georgia's state government revealed the profound instability of political power in a frontier region torn between Loyalism, Patriot factionalism, and Cherokee raids from the interior. His controversial 1777 presidency and bitter rivalry with General Lachlan McIntosh highlight the often-overlooked complexity of the Southern Revolution, where independence was a prolonged negotiation among fractious local elites rather than a unified popular rising. Gwinnett's death in a duel on May 19, 1777, made him one of the shortest-lived signers and left his autograph among the rarest in American historical collections.

## Early Life and Entry into Georgia Politics

Gwinnett was born in 1735 in Gloucester, England, to a family of modest means. He entered merchant life and by the early 1760s was trading in the West Indies. In 1762, seeking to improve his fortune, he emigrated to North America and established himself in St. Catherine's Parish, Georgia, about thirty miles south of Savannah. He acquired land (ultimately approximately 2,100 acres) and enslaved laborers, committing himself to plantation agriculture — a venture that would define his wealth and his politics.

Georgia in 1773–1775 was economically and politically marginal among the colonies. Its total population was under 30,000 (including roughly 15,000 enslaved people), its landed gentry was small and often contentious, and its economy depended heavily on rice and indigo cultivation linked to British export markets. The Revolutionary stirrings of the northern colonies reached Georgia slowly; it sent no delegation to the First Continental Congress in 1774 and was represented only by Lyman Hall at the Second in May 1775 — and Hall attended unofficially, from a single parish rather than from the full colony.

Gwinnett, as a newer settler and a merchant with trading connections in the West Indies, nonetheless became aware of the patriot network and was elected to Georgia's Provincial Congress (the de facto revolutionary government) in 1775. He was initially a moderate who advocated caution and possible reconciliation, but was convinced by the logic of independence as Parliament passed the Prohibitory Act in December 1775 (see [Prohibitory Act 1776]({{ '/news/economics/prohibitory-act-1776/' | relative_url }})) — which placed all American shipping outside the king's protection and effectively declared war on colonial commerce. By early 1776, Gwinnett had aligned with the independence faction.

## Georgia's Path to Independence

Georgia's entry into the Revolutionary coalition was the most hesitant of any colony. At the outbreak of hostilities in 1775, the colony's Royal Governor James Wright remained in office and actively resisted patriot organizing; he was arrested by the Council of Safety in January 1776 and escaped to a British warship in February, but his departure did not eliminate Loyalist sentiment. British naval vessels continued to operate in Georgia's coastal waters, and Savannah's merchant community remained divided.

The [Halifax Resolves]({{ '/news/politics/halifax-resolves-1776/' | relative_url }}) of April 12, 1776, passed by North Carolina's Provincial Congress, were the first formal colonial instruction to vote for independence — a signal that the entire Southern Confederation (Virginia, the Carolinas, Georgia) was turning. Virginia's corresponding resolutions in May 1776 gave the Continental Congress sufficient mandate for Richard Henry Lee's independence resolution of June 7. Georgia's Provincial Congress, meeting in Savannah, authorized its delegates to support independence in the weeks following the Lee Resolution of July 2 (see [Lee Resolution]({{ '/news/politics/lee-resolution/' | relative_url }})).

Gwinnett and Walton did not physically arrive at Congress in Philadelphia until late July or early August 1776; the [Declaration of Independence]({{ '/news/politics/declaration-of-independence/' | relative_url }}) had been adopted by Congress on July 4 and the engrossed parchment signed beginning August 2. All three Georgia delegates — Gwinnett, Hall, and Walton — signed the parchment in August 1776, making them among the last to formally sign. By placing his name on the document, Gwinnett accepted the legal definition of treason against the Crown and the penalty of execution if captured.

## The Rarity of Gwinnett's Autograph

Gwinnett's early death in 1777, combined with his relatively brief public career, means that fewer than three dozen authenticated examples of his signature are known to survive as of the twentieth century. As of 1927, only thirty-nine examples were documented; by 2010, careful scholarship had reduced this to roughly thirty-six confirmed authentic specimens. This scarcity has made his autograph among the most valuable in American history: a signed document sold at auction in 1927 for $51,000 (approximately $900,000 in 2024 dollars); more recent authenticated examples have exceeded $500,000. In contrast, John Hancock's signature — his being one of the most common among the signers — can be acquired for a few hundred dollars.

The contrast in autograph rarity vividly illustrates the disparate trajectories of the fifty-six signers: Gwinnett's revolutionary career lasted barely two years from first election to death, while men like [John Adams]({{ '/news/people/john-adams-1776/' | relative_url }}), [Thomas Jefferson]({{ '/news/people/thomas-jefferson-1776/' | relative_url }}), and [Benjamin Franklin]({{ '/news/people/benjamin-franklin-1776/' | relative_url }}) went on to decades of public correspondence.

## Revolutionary Leadership and Internal Conflict

After signing the Declaration, Gwinnett returned to Georgia and was elected to the state's new House of Assembly under the provisional constitution adopted in February 1777. In January 1777, he was elected President (chief executive) of Georgia — a position roughly equivalent to governor under the 1777 state constitution. His presidency was controversial and brief.

The central conflict of Gwinnett's presidency was his bitter rivalry with General Lachlan McIntosh, commander of the Continental Army forces in Georgia. McIntosh, a Scottish-born planter with deep roots in the colonial gentry, resented Gwinnett's authority over military affairs and accused him of incompetent management of a proposed invasion of East Florida (then a British colony). Gwinnett, for his part, suspected McIntosh of sabotaging the Florida expedition to embarrass him politically. The invasion, launched in the spring of 1777, was a failure: American forces penetrated to the St. Mary's River (the boundary with East Florida) but were repulsed and returned to Georgia without achieving their objective. McIntosh publicly called Gwinnett "a scoundrel and a lying rascal" on the floor of the Georgia Assembly, the customary fighting words that required a duel.

Gwinnett accepted the challenge. The two men met at dawn on May 16, 1777, at a plantation near Savannah. Both fired simultaneously at a range of about twelve feet. Both were hit: McIntosh in the thigh; Gwinnett in the leg, with the ball shattering his thigh bone above the knee. McIntosh recovered; Gwinnett did not. He died of his wound on May 19, 1777, at age 42 — three days after the encounter — from a gangrenous infection, while McIntosh recovered from his own wound.

## The Cherokee Raids and Southern War Context

Gwinnett's presidency coincided with the most intense phase of the [Cherokee War of 1776]({{ '/news/military/cherokee-war-1776/' | relative_url }}), in which Cherokee warriors allied with British agents attacked frontier settlements across the Carolinas and Georgia. In Georgia, the attacks struck the backcountry settlements west of Augusta, where Cherokee and Creek warriors raided farms and killed settlers throughout the summer of 1776. Georgia's Provincial Congress authorized punitive expeditions into Cherokee territory; South Carolina, North Carolina, and Virginia launched coordinated campaigns that resulted in the destruction of dozens of Cherokee towns and forced the Cherokee to sign the Treaty of DeWitt's Corner (May 1777) and the Treaty of Long Island of the Holston (July 1777) — surrendering large portions of their territory.

For Gwinnett as Georgia's leader, the Cherokee war represented one of three simultaneous threats: British coastal raids, Loyalist militias in the interior, and Cherokee warfare on the western frontier. His state government had minimal revenue (Continental paper currency was already losing value rapidly under the strains documented in [Continental Congress Finance 1776]({{ '/news/economics/continental-congress-finance-1776/' | relative_url }})), a tiny Continental Army contingent, and an unreliable militia. The coordination failures that plagued the Florida expedition reflected these structural weaknesses rather than solely personal incompetence.

## Legacy and Historiographical Place

Gwinnett's death foreclosed the longer career that might have given him the historical prominence of his fellow Georgia signers Hall and Walton. He remained relatively obscure in national memory — partly because Georgia's Revolutionary history has been overshadowed by the Northern-focused narrative of 1775–1777, and partly because his brief presidency ended in failure and his death came in a dispute that seemed more personal than political.

Yet Gwinnett's experience illuminates crucial dimensions of the American founding: the diversity of circumstances faced by the fifty-six signers, the fragility of revolutionary solidarity in the Southern colonies, the real costs (not merely symbolic) borne by those who signed, and the complexity of building state governments simultaneously with fighting a war. In the context of 1776 itself, Gwinnett's story — merchant-planter to provincial congressman to independence signer to embattled executive to dueling fatality — is among the most compressed and consequential of any figure directly involved in the Declaration.

His name today is primarily preserved in Gwinnett County, Georgia (established 1818), one of the most populous counties in the modern state.
