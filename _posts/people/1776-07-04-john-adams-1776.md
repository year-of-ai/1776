**Key figures**: John Adams, Thomas Jefferson, Benjamin Franklin, Roger Sherman, Robert R. Livingston

## Summary

John Adams served as a Massachusetts delegate to the Second Continental Congress in 1776, emerging as one of the most influential voices advocating for American independence. In June, Congress appointed him to the Committee of Five to draft the Declaration of Independence, alongside Jefferson, Franklin, Sherman, and Livingston. Though Jefferson authored the initial draft, Adams played a decisive role in the committee's deliberations and, during Congressional debate on July 1–4, argued vigorously for the Declaration's adoption—a role for which Jefferson later praised him as "the pillar of [the Declaration's] support." Beyond the Declaration, Adams undertook an extraordinary Congressional workload: he served on ninety committees, chairing twenty-five, including leadership of the Board of War and Ordnance where he effectively functioned as a *de facto* Secretary of War, managing military logistics and strategy for the Revolutionary cause.

## The Committee of Five and the Declaration

On June 7, 1776, Richard Henry Lee of Virginia introduced a formal resolution in Congress calling for independence. Congress appointed the Committee of Five on June 11 to draft a declaration supporting Lee's resolution. Adams deferred the primary authorship to Jefferson, later recalling that he had given Jefferson three reasons: that a Virginian "ought to appear at the head of this business," that Adams himself was "obnoxious, suspected, and unpopular" while Jefferson was not, and that Jefferson could "write ten times better" than he could. Over approximately two weeks (June 11–28), Jefferson produced the draft while Adams and Franklin made only minor stylistic revisions—the change of "sacred and undeniable" truths to "self-evident," often attributed to Franklin, produced one of the document's most famous phrases.

During the July 1–4 Congressional debates, Adams delivered the most forceful floor speeches in favor of adoption. He refuted John Dickinson's conciliation arguments point by point. New York abstained, Delaware was split (Caesar Rodney's midnight ride on July 1 broke the tie), and South Carolina initially opposed but reversed position on July 2. The vote on Lee's resolution for independence passed on July 2, 1776—the date Adams predicted would be "the most memorable epocha in the history of America." Congress formally adopted the edited Declaration on July 4.

## Congressional Leadership and the Board of War

Adams' influence in 1776 extended far beyond the Declaration. He served on the five-member Marine Committee beginning in November 1775, overseeing the nascent Continental Navy, and in June 1776 he drafted the first plan for a Model Treaty—a template for foreign alliances that eschewed "entangling" political commitments in favor of commercial arrangements, influencing U.S. diplomatic philosophy for generations.

His most consequential committee role was chairing the Board of War and Ordnance, established July 12, 1776. The Board coordinated troop recruitment, weapons procurement, and strategic planning across thirteen separate colonial militias—a logistical challenge with no precedent. Adams managed correspondence with General Washington, negotiated with suppliers for gunpowder and cannon, and reviewed courts-martial. Between January and December 1776, he wrote an estimated 500 letters on board business alone, a record of administrative output that earned him the informal title of "Atlas of Independence" among contemporaries.

## Personal Circumstances and Correspondence

Adams maintained a celebrated wartime correspondence with his wife Abigail Adams from Philadelphia. Her March 31, 1776, letter—in which she famously urged him to "remember the Ladies"—captured the transformative social debates stirred by revolution. Adams replied flippantly, but historians regard the exchange as an early document of American feminist thought. Adams was absent for the birth of a stillborn daughter (July 1777) and spent long stretches separated from his family at Braintree, Massachusetts.

He returned briefly to Massachusetts in September 1776 to participate in the state convention drafting the Massachusetts constitution—one of the first such constitutions in the new republic, serving as a model for later state and federal documents.

## Significance

Adams' work in 1776 established him as a principal architect of American independence. His relentless advocacy for separation from Great Britain—beginning in autumn 1775 when "no one in Congress labored more ardently than Adams to hasten the day when America would be separate from Great Britain"—shifted the political momentum from reconciliation to revolution. His roles in drafting, defending, and institutionalizing independence through military oversight positioned him as a founding father whose intellectual and organizational contributions were essential to the birth of the United States. His work at the Continental Congress in 1776 launched his subsequent diplomatic career and political ascendancy, culminating in his election as the second U.S. President in 1796.
