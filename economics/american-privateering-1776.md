# American Privateering Commissions and Early Operations in 1776

**Category**: Economics & Trade  
**Key figures**: Benjamin Franklin, John Adams, Continental Congress, Commodore Esek Hopkins, privateering captains and merchant owners

## Summary

Facing the overwhelming naval superiority of the British Royal Navy, the Continental Congress legalized and authorized privateering on March 23, 1776, and issued formal instructions to privateer commanders on April 3, 1776. These legal commissions transformed American merchant ships into licensed raiders authorized to capture British commercial vessels. Unlike Continental Navy crews, who received limited pay and shared only one-third of prize money, privateers kept substantial portions of seized cargo and ship value, creating powerful financial incentives for merchants, whalers, and fishermen to convert their vessels into warships. By May 1776, approximately 100 New England privateers were already operating in Caribbean waters, demonstrating rapid adoption of the strategy. Letters of Marque authorized armed merchant vessels on a per-voyage basis, while permanent privateer commissions designated specialized commerce raiders. This hybrid approach—blending patriotic support for independence with financial self-interest—proved far more effective than the Continental Navy alone, eventually capturing approximately 2,300 British merchant vessels over the war's duration.

## Legal Framework and Authorization

Congress's March 23, 1776 resolution built upon centuries of maritime precedent. A **Letter of Marque and Reprisal** gave a captain state authority to capture enemy vessels and goods; a **Privateer Commission** granted broader standing authority for dedicated commerce raiders. The April 3, 1776 Instructions to Privateers (drafted largely by John Adams) established strict rules of engagement: no plundering neutral ships, humane treatment of prisoners, and mandatory posting of bonds ranging from $5,000 to $10,000 to deter abuses. Captured prizes were brought to American ports where admiralty courts adjudicated their legality. The crew typically received 100% of the prize value — a stark contrast to Continental Navy regulations that sent one-third to Congress.

The prize distribution formula was carefully calibrated to maximize recruitment:

- Captain and officers: approximately 40% of the prize value
- Crew: approximately 60% distributed proportionally by rank and role
- Ship owners: retained the vessel and received a commission on prize proceeds

Congress granted commissions to vessels of all sizes. Schooners of 50–150 tons were most common in 1776, carrying 6–14 guns and crews of 30–80 men. By year's end, 136 commissions had been issued, and colonial legislatures added hundreds more under their own authority.

## Key Operations and Vessels in 1776

The privateer fleet inflicted immediate damage on British commerce. Several engagements stood out in 1776:

- **Spring 1776**: The line between the official navy and private raiders was initially blurred — early captures such as the Continental brig *Lexington*'s taking of the British tender *Edward* off the Delaware Capes on April 7, 1776 were made by commissioned Continental cruisers, while the far larger privateer fleet was still fitting out in New England ports.
- **May–June 1776**: New England privateers working Caribbean sea lanes seized dozens of British provision ships bound for Crown forces in North America, creating supply shortages that complicated British military planning.
- **Summer 1776**: The sloop *Lady Washington* (later renamed *General Washington*) and vessels such as the *Yankee Hero* took British prizes worth tens of thousands of pounds, sending ripples through London's marine insurance markets.
- **Late 1776**: Some privateers operated as far as the waters off the British Isles, a pattern that would escalate dramatically after the French alliance in 1778.

Ports most active in issuing and outfitting privateers included Salem, Marblehead, Beverly, Boston, Providence, New London, and Philadelphia. Salem alone would commission over 150 privateers during the entire war.

## Financial Impact and Prize Economy

The prize economy had significant downstream effects on American finance. Captured goods — naval stores, food, cloth, weapons, and luxury items — entered American markets and partially offset the severe shortages caused by the British blockade authorized in the [Prohibitory Act](prohibitory-act-1776.md). Prize cargoes were auctioned at port, generating revenue that circulated through the colonial economy at a time when [Continental paper currency](continental-congress-finance-1776.md) was rapidly depreciating.

Insurance rates for British merchant vessels sailing the Atlantic soared from roughly 2–3% in peacetime to 15–25% and higher by mid-1776, devastating the profitability of trans-Atlantic trade. The Bank of England's records show that by late 1776, underwriters were refusing to cover certain routes without military convoy escort. This economic pressure aligned with Benjamin Franklin's diplomatic messaging in Paris: he circulated prize lists and accounts of captured British vessels to French merchants and ministers as evidence of American maritime effectiveness, strengthening the case for French commercial partnership and eventual alliance (see [French Alliance & Diplomacy](../politics/french-alliance-1778.md)).

Privately, Congress recognized that privateering partially solved the manpower problem that plagued the [Continental Navy](../military/continental-navy-1776.md). Naval enlistment was difficult because Continental sailors earned substantially less than privateers and lacked the windfall prize incentive. Some historians estimate that 10–15 times as many American sailors served on privateers as in the Continental Navy throughout the war.

## Relationship to Continental Military Strategy

Congress and General Washington held ambivalent views on privateering. Washington worried that privateering drained skilled sailors and potential officers from the Continental Navy and the army. In several letters of 1776 he urged restraint, noting that a man motivated purely by prize money would desert if the prizes dried up. John Adams countered that the privateers' self-financing nature was their primary virtue: Congress could not afford to build, arm, and pay for a fleet sufficient to challenge British naval power, but could issue paper commissions at minimal cost.

The two systems were complementary rather than competing. The Continental Navy (see [Continental Navy Establishment and Operations in 1776](../military/continental-navy-1776.md)) pursued strategic goals — raiding British home ports, escorting diplomatic missions, and protecting American harbors — while privateers focused almost exclusively on capturing commercial prizes. When the Raid of Nassau recovered cannon and powder in March 1776, Commodore Hopkins's naval force was performing missions no privateer captain would risk for a non-monetary strategic objective.

Beaumarchais's covert supply operation [Roderigue Hortalez et Cie](roderigue-hortalez-company.md), which funneled French and Spanish arms to America through a fictitious trading house, intersected with the privateering economy: some ships carrying French war material used privateer-style documentation to evade British seizure, blurring the line between government supply and commercial raiding.

## Legacy of the 1776 Authorization

The March 1776 authorization established the legal and institutional template for American commerce raiding. By the war's end, an estimated 55,000 Americans had served on privateers — more than had served in the Continental Army at any one moment — and approximately 2,300 British merchant vessels were captured or destroyed. The prize economy transferred an estimated £18 million in British commercial wealth to American hands, a sum exceeding total French subsidies received before 1778.

The 1776 framework also shaped American legal tradition: U.S. admiralty law, prize court procedures, and the constitutional provision granting Congress power to "grant Letters of Marque and Reprisal" (Article I, Section 8) all derive directly from the precedents established in 1776. The United States declined to ratify the 1856 Declaration of Paris abolishing privateering, leaving the option theoretically available until the 20th century.
