# Edward Gibbon's The History of the Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire (Vol. 1)

**Category**: Arts & Culture
**Key figures**: Edward Gibbon, William Strahan, Thomas Cadell, David Hume

## Summary

Edward Gibbon was born on May 8, 1737, in Putney, Surrey, England. His formal education was irregular: a period at Magdalen College, Oxford, which he later criticized as indifferent to learning, was cut short when his father sent him to Lausanne, Switzerland, after Gibbon briefly converted to Catholicism. In Lausanne he studied under the Calvinist minister David Pavillard, recanted his conversion, and encountered the intellectual currents of the French Enlightenment, including Voltaire, whom he met in person, and the works of Montesquieu. He served in the Hampshire militia during the Seven Years' War and sat in the House of Commons from 1774 as a silent supporter of Lord North's ministry. His concept for the work that would become *The Decline and Fall* was formed, by his own account, while sitting amid the ruins of the Capitol in Rome in 1764, when he resolved to write the history of the city's fall.

The first volume of *The History of the Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire* was published on February 17, 1776, by William Strahan and Thomas Cadell in London. The volume covered the period from the height of the Roman Empire under the Antonine emperors in the second century AD through the establishment of the Western Roman Empire under Diocletian in the late third century. Gibbon organized his narrative around a central thesis: that Rome declined because of the gradual erosion of civic virtue among its citizens, compounded by external barbarian pressure and, controversially, the rise of Christianity. The final two chapters of the volume — chapters 15 and 16 — examined the early spread of Christianity with the analytical detachment Gibbon applied to all historical phenomena, questioning traditional Church accounts of the number of Christian martyrs and attributing the religion's growth to secondary human causes rather than divine providence.

The first volume was received with widespread critical praise, and Gibbon's contemporary David Hume described it as a landmark of the historical age. The work immediately sold out its first print run and required rapid reprints. However, the treatment of Christianity in chapters 15 and 16 provoked a wave of clerical controversy. Gibbon was attacked by several Church of England clergy and responded with his *Vindication* in 1779, which the historian Hugh Trevor-Roper later called "one of the decisive battles of literary history." The historian drew on Enlightenment thinkers including Montesquieu, Voltaire, and Bossuet, and was distinctive in going back to primary sources — ancient texts — rather than relying on the accepted secondary tradition of Church history. He completed the work in six volumes, with Volumes II and III published in 1781 and Volumes IV through VI in 1788, covering the history of Rome from the third century through the fall of Constantinople in 1453.

Gibbon's methodology represented a significant advance in English historical writing. He systematically cited his sources in footnotes, a practice not universal in 18th-century historical works. He approached ecclesiastical history with the same empirical skepticism he applied to political and military events, privileging documentary evidence over hagiography. His style — celebrated for its irony, precision, and sustained rhetorical control over vast narrative spans — was itself a product of his Enlightenment formation, and the work became a model for subsequent scholarly historical prose in English.

## Significance

The first volume of *The Decline and Fall*, published in February 1776, inaugurated what historians credit as the modern study of Roman history in the English-speaking world. Gibbon's combination of wide documentary research, rationalist critical method, and high literary style set a standard that shaped historical writing for generations. The work's argument that internal moral decay and religious transformation contributed to Rome's fall entered Western political and intellectual discourse as a persistent reference point. While modern historians largely reject his monocausal emphasis on Christianity, they credit him with initiating comparative and secular approaches to ancient history. The controversy the work generated in 1776 and beyond reflected the tension between Enlightenment rationalism and established religion that characterized the intellectual culture of the period.
