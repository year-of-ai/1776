# Battle of Moore's Creek Bridge

**Category**: Military Events
**Key figures**: Richard Caswell, Alexander Lillington, Donald MacDonald, Donald McLeod

## Summary

The Battle of Moore's Creek Bridge was fought on February 27, 1776, near Wilmington in what is now Pender County, North Carolina. It arose when North Carolina's royal governor, Josiah Martin, called on Loyalists to muster and march to the coast to link up with a planned British seaborne expedition intended to restore royal authority in the southern colonies. Roughly 1,600 Loyalists assembled under Brigadier General Donald MacDonald, the large majority Scottish Highland immigrants from the Cape Fear Valley, joined by former Regulators; by the time the column reached Moore's Creek, MacDonald had fallen ill and desertions had thinned the force to somewhere between 700 and 800 men, with command passing to Lieutenant Colonel Donald McLeod.

Patriot militia, numbering roughly 1,000 to 1,050 men under Colonels Richard Caswell and Alexander Lillington, moved to block the Loyalists' path at the bridge over Moore's Creek. The Patriots entrenched on the east bank, partially removed the bridge's planking, and greased the exposed support stringers to slow any crossing, while positioning two small cannon to cover the span. When the Loyalists advanced at dawn on February 27, McLeod led an advance party in a broadsword charge across the slick, partially bare bridge; the exposed attackers were met with concentrated Patriot musket and cannon fire and the charge collapsed within moments. McLeod himself was killed in the opening volleys, struck by numerous rounds, and the engagement — among the shortest of the Revolutionary War — was decided within minutes.

Patriot losses were minimal, variously reported as one or two killed and a similarly small number wounded. Loyalist casualties were far heavier, with roughly 30 killed outright and dozens more wounded; in the days following the battle, Patriot forces rounded up several hundred Loyalist prisoners, along with supplies and weapons, effectively dismantling the Loyalist column as a fighting force.

## Significance

Moore's Creek Bridge is widely regarded as the first significant Patriot battlefield victory of the American Revolutionary War and is sometimes called "the Lexington and Concord of the South" for its role in galvanizing revolutionary sentiment in that region. The defeat destroyed organized Loyalist military strength in North Carolina, forced the collapse of Governor Martin's plan to reassert royal control, and discouraged Loyalist recruitment across the southern colonies for years afterward, delaying Britain's southern campaign strategy.

The victory also had immediate political consequences: emboldened by the removal of the internal Loyalist threat, North Carolina's Fourth Provincial Congress convened at Halifax and, on April 12, 1776, adopted the Halifax Resolves, the first formal colony-wide authorization for delegates to vote for independence in the Continental Congress — a measure that helped pave the way toward the Declaration of Independence three months later.
