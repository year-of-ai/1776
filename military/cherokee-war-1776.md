# The Cherokee War of 1776

**Category**: Military Events
**Key figures**: Dragging Canoe, Oconostota, Attakullakulla, Nancy Ward, John Stuart, Griffith Rutherford, Andrew Williamson, William Christian

## Summary

The Cherokee War of 1776 was a coordinated series of attacks by Cherokee war parties on the Watauga, Holston, Carolina, and Georgia frontiers beginning in late June and July 1776, followed by devastating Patriot punitive expeditions that summer and fall. Provoked by the 1775 Treaty of Sycamore Shoals, in which older headmen ceded some 27,000 square miles of Cherokee hunting ground to the Transylvania Land Company, the younger war leader Dragging Canoe rejected the cession and led simultaneous strikes against frontier forts and settlements just as the Second Continental Congress was declaring independence. The campaign opened what historians call the Cherokee–American wars, a conflict that would not fully end until 1794.

Although Cherokee forces achieved early momentum, the colonies' response was overwhelming and decisive. Massive militia armies from North Carolina, South Carolina, Virginia, and Georgia marched into Cherokee territory in August and September 1776, burning dozens of Overhill, Middle, Valley, and Lower towns along with their stored corn. The devastation forced most Cherokee leadership to sue for peace in 1777, ceding still more land, while Dragging Canoe and his followers broke away to continue resistance from new towns on Chickamauga Creek near present-day Chattanooga.

## The July 1776 Attacks

Cherokee war parties from the Lower Towns began raiding the South Carolina frontier in late June 1776, and by July 1 war parties from the Out, Middle, and Valley Towns had struck settlements on the North Carolina frontier east of the Blue Ridge. Nancy Ward, the Cherokee Beloved Woman and a relative of Dragging Canoe, sent warning of the impending attacks to nearby settlers, allowing some to reach the safety of frontier forts. On July 20, 1776, Dragging Canoe led roughly 160 warriors against Virginia and North Carolina militia near the Long Island of the Holston, in an engagement sometimes called the Battle of Island Flats, and was wounded in the hip. That same period, other Cherokee forces under Abraham of Chilhowee besieged Fort Watauga (Fort Caswell) for about two weeks before withdrawing, while parties on the Catawba River in North Carolina and around Black's Fort in southwestern Virginia killed dozens of settlers. British Superintendent of Indian Affairs John Stuart, along with his deputies Alexander Cameron and his brother Henry Stuart, had urged the Cherokee to avoid open war for fear of provoking exactly this response, but nonetheless supplied ammunition and support once fighting began, feeding Patriot suspicions of a British-incited frontier war.

## The Patriot Punitive Expeditions

Colonial governments responded with coordinated, large-scale retaliation over August and September 1776. Brigadier General Griffith Rutherford led a North Carolina militia force reported at roughly 2,400 men across the mountains into the Middle and Valley Towns, destroying dozens of settlements. Colonel Andrew Williamson led South Carolina and Georgia militia, joined by officers including Andrew Pickens, against the Lower Towns, burning towns such as Keowee, Seneca, Tugaloo, and Tamassee along with their orchards and food stores. Colonel William Christian led a Virginia force, reported at around 1,100 to 1,800 men, into the Overhill Towns along the Little Tennessee River, finding many towns, including the influential town of Chota, already abandoned and burned by their own inhabitants in anticipation of the invasion. Across the combined campaigns, Patriot forces destroyed more than fifty Cherokee towns and vast stores of corn, killing livestock and rendering thousands of Cherokee homeless and without food for the coming winter.

## Aftermath and the Chickamauga Breakaway

Facing catastrophic losses, most Cherokee leadership, including the elder statesmen Attakullakulla and Oconostota, sought peace. Cherokee delegations negotiated the Treaty of DeWitt's Corner with South Carolina and Georgia (May 20, 1777) and the Treaty of Long Island of Holston with Virginia and North Carolina (July 1777), both of which forced further large cessions of Cherokee land. Dragging Canoe refused to accept the terms, declaring that he and his warriors would hold their remaining lands, and led a militant faction away from the Overhill Towns to found new settlements on Chickamauga Creek near present-day Chattanooga, Tennessee. From these "Chickamauga towns," Dragging Canoe's followers, later called the Chickamauga Cherokee, continued independent raids against American settlements for roughly another seventeen years, sustaining the broader Cherokee–American wars into the 1790s.

## Significance

The Cherokee War of 1776 opened the longest-running Native American conflict of the Revolutionary era and demonstrated, within weeks of American independence, the scale of frontier violence the new nation would wage against Indigenous nations that resisted encroachment. The scorched-earth Patriot campaigns broke the Cherokee's capacity for unified resistance and produced treaty cessions that permanently reduced Cherokee territory in the Carolinas, while Dragging Canoe's Chickamauga breakaway ensured that armed resistance persisted on the southern frontier throughout the Revolutionary War and beyond.
