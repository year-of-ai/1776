# Battle of Long Island

**Category**: Military Events
**Key figures**: George Washington, William Howe, Henry Clinton, Lord Stirling (William Alexander), Israel Putnam, John Sullivan, Charles Cornwallis

## Summary

The Battle of Long Island, also called the Battle of Brooklyn, was fought on August 27, 1776, in Brooklyn, New York. It was the largest engagement of the American Revolutionary War by troop numbers, pitting approximately 20,000 British and Hessian troops under General William Howe against around 10,000 Continental soldiers under General George Washington. The battle opened the British campaign to seize New York City and was the first major engagement after the Declaration of Independence.

British commanders executed a decisive nighttime flanking maneuver through the Jamaica Pass on the night of August 26–27. While diversionary forces pinned American attention along the Gowanus Road, General Henry Clinton led approximately 10,000 soldiers through the lightly guarded Jamaica Pass and swung behind the American left flank, reaching the village of Bedford by mid-morning. The encircling movement compressed the American forward positions between two British forces. Generals John Sullivan and Lord Stirling (William Alexander) commanded the exposed American flanks. Stirling detached approximately 260–270 Maryland soldiers — the "Maryland 400" — in a series of rear-guard charges against Cornwallis's troops at the Old Stone House (Vechte–Cortelyou House), enabling thousands of Continental soldiers to escape across Gowanus Creek. The Maryland regiment suffered catastrophic losses, with units losing up to 80 percent of their men. Washington, observing the action from a rise, reportedly said he feared the day was lost.

Howe chose not to assault the American lines at Brooklyn Heights following the field victory, instead beginning siege preparations. This pause, and a fortuitous fog on the night of August 29–30, gave Washington the opportunity to conduct a masterful evacuation. Using flat-bottomed boats and maintaining strict silence among his troops, Washington moved approximately 9,000 soldiers across the East River to Manhattan without a single casualty or loss of supplies. Washington himself was among the last to leave Brooklyn. Total American casualties for the battle were approximately 300 killed, 800 wounded, and 1,079 captured or missing; British and Hessian losses were around 64 killed, 293 wounded, and 31 missing.

## Significance

The Battle of Long Island demonstrated both the vulnerability of Washington's Continental Army and his capacity for strategic improvisation under pressure. The British tactical victory gave Howe control of Long Island and set the stage for the fall of New York City, which remained under British occupation for the remainder of the Revolutionary War. The nighttime evacuation across the East River — often compared to the Dunkirk evacuation for its preservation of a battered force — was widely recognized as Washington's greatest early-war decision; it kept the Continental Army intact and in the field. The battle also revealed persistent American weaknesses in reconnaissance, particularly the failure to adequately guard the Jamaica Pass. The sacrifice of the Maryland regiment at the Old Stone House became one of the war's celebrated acts of rear-guard courage and is commemorated at the site in Brooklyn's Prospect Park today.
