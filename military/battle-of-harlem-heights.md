# Battle of Harlem Heights

**Category**: Military Events | **Date**: September 16, 1776
**Key figures**: George Washington, Thomas Knowlton, Henry Clinton, William Howe

## Summary

The Battle of Harlem Heights was a pivotal engagement fought on September 16, 1776, in the Morningside Heights and Harlem neighborhoods of Manhattan during the American Revolutionary War. Commanded by General George Washington, approximately 1,800 Continental troops repulsed a British force of about 1,600 soldiers under General Henry Clinton. The battle began as a skirmish between Knowlton's Rangers and British light infantry pickets that escalated into sustained combat as Washington committed additional forces. After roughly two hours of fighting in open fields near present-day Barnard College and Columbia University, the British — running low on ammunition — withdrew to their lines. The American victory was Washington's first tactical success of the war, coming just three weeks after the catastrophic defeat at the [Battle of Long Island](battle-of-long-island.md).

## Strategic Context: Manhattan, September 1776

The battle occurred during a desperate phase for American forces. Following the [Battle of Long Island](battle-of-long-island.md) on August 27–29, 1776 — the largest engagement of the entire Revolutionary War — Washington had executed a skillful nighttime evacuation of some 9,000 troops across the East River to Manhattan, but British forces now controlled Long Island and were poised to assault Manhattan itself.

On September 15, 1776 — the day before Harlem Heights — British and Hessian troops landed at Kip's Bay on the eastern shore of Manhattan (near present-day 34th Street) with overwhelming naval gunfire support. Continental troops fled in disorder, and Washington rode among the retreating soldiers in fury, reportedly striking officers with his riding crop. By nightfall on September 15, Washington had retreated with most of his army to Harlem Heights, an elevated ridge running roughly from present-day 125th Street to 155th Street. The high ground offered natural defensive advantage, and Washington established his headquarters at the Roger Morris House (now the Morris-Jumel Mansion).

The situation on the morning of September 16 was grim: morale was at its lowest point of the war, desertion rates were rising, and many soldiers' enlistments were set to expire by year's end.

## Course of the Battle

### The Morning Skirmish

Before dawn on September 16, Washington ordered Colonel Thomas Knowlton — commander of an elite reconnaissance unit known as Knowlton's Rangers, drawn primarily from Connecticut troops — to probe British positions south of Harlem Heights. Knowlton's force of approximately 150 men moved south through the Hollow Way, a depression between the American and British lines, and made contact with the British 2nd Light Infantry Battalion under Lieutenant Colonel Robert Abercrombie around 7:00 a.m.

Outnumbered, Knowlton's Rangers initially withdrew northward with the British in pursuit. As the British light infantry crested the ridge in view of Washington and his staff, they blew their bugles in a fox-hunt call (*the "gone to earth" or "View Halloo"*) — the signal hunters use when a fox has gone to ground — a deliberate taunt implying the retreating Americans were a frightened quarry. Washington, incensed by the insult, immediately resolved to turn the tables.

### Washington's Counterattack

Washington dispatched three coordinated forces:

1. **Feint force** (~150 men under Major Andrew Leitch): Advance to fix the British light infantry in place by direct engagement.
2. **Flanking force** (~230 Rangers and Connecticut troops under Knowlton): Circle west through woodland to cut off the British retreat.
3. **Reserve force** (~800 men under various officers): Stand ready to exploit success.

The plan was for the flanking force to get behind the British before the feint opened fire, but a premature engagement by the feint force triggered the action before Knowlton's rangers reached their flanking position. Nevertheless, British troops facing fire from two directions began to fall back.

Colonel Knowlton was struck by a musket ball during the flanking maneuver and died of his wound that afternoon — one of the most significant American losses of the engagement. Major Leitch was also wounded early in the fighting.

### Afternoon Fighting and British Withdrawal

Despite losing both commanders early in the action, American troops pressed the advantage with renewed aggression. Washington fed additional regiments — including Virginia and Maryland troops — into the fight. The engagement expanded southward into open buckwheat fields and orchards near present-day Barnard College.

The British, initially reinforced by two additional battalions (including Hessian troops), eventually found themselves running low on ammunition after two hours of sustained combat. Rather than risk encirclement, General Clinton ordered withdrawal southward to the main British lines near present-day 96th Street, leaving the Americans in possession of the field.

Washington halted pursuit at the main British defensive lines, unwilling to risk a general engagement against the full British army.

## Casualties and Aftermath

| Force | Killed | Wounded | Total |
|---|---|---|---|
| Continental Army | ~30 | ~100 | ~130 |
| British/Hessian forces | ~14 | ~157 | ~171 |

The casualty disparity reinforced the psychological impact: Continental troops had inflicted more casualties than they suffered against professional British soldiers in open field combat — a powerful counter to the narrative of American incompetence that had followed Long Island.

Washington's general orders the following day praised the troops in unusually warm terms, noting that "the behaviour of yesterday was such a contrast to that of some troops the day before, that it greatly animated the army." Morale, which had collapsed at Kip's Bay just 24 hours earlier, rebounded significantly.

## Significance

The Battle of Harlem Heights held extraordinary symbolic and military importance for the Continental Army:

- **Washington's first victory**: It marked his first successful engagement of the Revolutionary War, coming after defeats at Long Island, Kip's Bay, and lesser skirmishes.
- **Proof of Continental capability**: The engagement demonstrated that American troops could fight, maneuver, and drive British regulars from a field — contradicting the assumption that only fully trained European-style professional soldiers could stand against the British Army.
- **Strategic stabilization**: The victory bought Washington time to consolidate his position on Manhattan before the eventual British flanking maneuver at Throg's Neck in October 1776 forced a further northward retreat.
- **Morale**: The psychological effect proved as important as the tactical outcome. The reinvigorated army went on to fight the [Battle of Trenton](battle-of-trenton.md) in December 1776 — the surprise crossing of the Delaware that reversed the entire strategic situation.

The death of Colonel Thomas Knowlton, sometimes called "the American Ajax," deprived Washington of one of his most capable reconnaissance commanders. Knowlton had earlier commanded at the Battle of Bunker Hill in June 1775 and had led the Rangers on a daring reconnaissance mission across Manhattan in early September 1776.
